6.4 The Binomial Theorem
Objective: Use Pascal's Triangle, combinations, and the Binomial Theorem to expand expressions and find specific terms.

Concept
Patterns in Binomial Expansions
Before using a formula, it helps to notice what always happens in an expansion of \((a+b)^n\).
Number of Terms
There are always \(n+1\) terms.
Exponents
The exponent on the first factor decreases while the exponent on the second factor increases.
Coefficients
The coefficients are symmetric and match Pascal's Triangle.
Sample Pattern
\[(a+b)^4 = a^4 + 4a^3b + 6a^2b^2 + 4ab^3 + b^4\]

Combination Formula
\[\binom{n}{r} = \dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\]
Calculator
On many calculators, use nCr to find \(\binom{n}{r}\).
Row 4 Example
Row \(4\) gives \(1, 4, 6, 4, 1\), which are the coefficients of \((a+b)^4\).
Example 1
Expanding with Pascal's Triangle
Use Pascal's Triangle to expand \((2x + y)^4\).
Choose the Row of Coefficients
\[1,\ 4,\ 6,\ 4,\ 1\]
Row \(4\) matches the exponent \(4\).
Match the Power Pattern
\[(2x)^4,\ (2x)^3y,\ (2x)^2y^2,\ (2x)y^3,\ y^4\]
Multiply the Coefficients In
\[(2x+y)^4 = 16x^4 + 32x^3y + 24x^2y^2 + 8xy^3 + y^4\]
Reference
The Binomial Theorem
theorem
\[(a+b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}a^{\,n-r}b^r\]
term
\[T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r}a^{\,n-r}b^r\]
The term number is always one more than \(r\), so the 6th term uses \(r=5\).
How to Find a Specific Term
  1. Set \(r = \text{term number} - 1\)
  2. Plug \(a\), \(b\), \(n\), \(r\) into \(T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r\)
  3. Simplify powers and coefficients separately
Example 2
Expanding with the Binomial Theorem
Use the Binomial Theorem to expand \((x - 3)^5\).
Identify \(a\), \(b\), and \(n\)
\[a = x,\qquad b = -3,\qquad n = 5\]
Apply the Theorem
\[(x-3)^5 = \sum_{r=0}^{5}\binom{5}{r}x^{5-r}(-3)^r\]
Simplify Each Term
\[(x-3)^5 = x^5 - 15x^4 + 90x^3 - 270x^2 + 405x - 243\]
Watch Out
When \(b\) is negative, the signs alternate: \((-b)^0 = +\), \((-b)^1 = -\), \((-b)^2 = +\), \((-b)^3 = -\), …
Example 3
Another Full Expansion
Write the expansion of \((3x+1)^5\).
Identify \(a\), \(b\), and \(n\)
\[a = 3x,\qquad b = 1,\qquad n = 5\]
Apply the Theorem
\[(3x)^5 + 5(3x)^4(1) + 10(3x)^3(1)^2 + 10(3x)^2(1)^3 + 5(3x)(1)^4 + 1\]
Simplify
\[(3x+1)^5 = 243x^5 + 405x^4 + 270x^3 + 90x^2 + 15x + 1\]
Example 4
Finding a Specific Term
Find the 6th term of \((2x - y)^8\).
Match the Term Number to \(r\)
\[\text{6th term} \Rightarrow r = 5\]
Use the General Term
\[T_6 = \binom{8}{5}(2x)^{8-5}(-y)^5\]
Simplify
\[T_6 = 56(2x)^3(-y)^5 = 56(8x^3)(-y^5) = -448x^3y^5\]
Example 5
Finding Another Specific Term
Find the 4th term of \((3x-5)^9\).
Match the Term Number to \(r\)
\[\text{4th term} \Rightarrow r = 3\]
Use the General Term
\[T_4 = \binom{9}{3}(3x)^{6}(-5)^3 = 84 \cdot 729x^6 \cdot (-125)\]
Answer
\[T_4 = -7{,}654{,}500\,x^6\]

Example 6
Finding a Coefficient
Find the coefficient of \(x^2\) in the expansion of \((2x + 3)^5\).
Identify Which Power of \(3\) You Need
\[x^2 \text{ means } 5-r = 2 \Rightarrow r = 3\]
Use the Matching Term
\[\binom{5}{3}(2x)^2(3)^3 = 10 \cdot 4x^2 \cdot 27\]
Answer
\[\text{Coefficient of }x^2 = 1080\]
Example 7
Two-Variable Coefficient
Find the coefficient of \(x^7 y^{12}\) in the expansion of \((2x-3y^3)^{11}\).
Set Up the General Term
\[T_{r+1} = \binom{11}{r}(2x)^{11-r}(-3y^3)^r\]
Match the Exponents
  • \(x\)-exponent: \(11-r = 7 \implies r = 4\)
  • \(y\)-exponent: \(3r = 12\) ✓
Substitute \(r = 4\)
\[\binom{11}{4}(2x)^7(-3y^3)^4 = 330 \cdot 128x^7 \cdot 81y^{12}\]
Answer
\[\text{Coefficient} = 3{,}421{,}440\]
