4.2 Radian and Degree Measure
Objective: Describe angles in standard position, use radian measure, and convert between degrees and radians.
standard position positive vs negative rotation coterminal angles radian / degree conversion complement & supplement
Concept
Standard Position
An angle is in standard position when its vertex sits at the origin and its initial side lies along the positive \(x\)-axis.
Standard-Position Angle
Vertex
The corner of the angle — always at the origin for a standard-position angle.
Initial Side
The fixed starting ray, lying along the positive \(x\)-axis.
Terminal Side
The ray that has been rotated from the initial side. Its position determines the angle's measure.
Concept
Direction of Rotation: Positive vs. Negative
Rotate the terminal side counterclockwise for a positive angle; clockwise for a negative angle.
Positive Angle (CCW) — 120°
Negative Angle (CW) — −145°
Counterclockwise → Positive
The terminal side rotates up and around from the initial side.
Clockwise → Negative
The terminal side rotates down and around from the initial side.
Concept
Coterminal Angles
Coterminal angles share the same terminal side. They differ from each other by full revolutions — \(360°\) in degree measure, or \(2\pi\) in radian measure.
Several Angles, Same Terminal Side
Degrees
\[\theta_{\text{coterminal}} = \theta \pm 360°\, k, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}\]
Radians
\[\theta_{\text{coterminal}} = \theta \pm 2\pi\, k, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}\]
In Practice
Add or subtract one full revolution at a time until you land in the range you want — or until you have a partner that's positive (or negative).
Example 1
Find Coterminal Angles
Find two angles — one positive and one negative — that are coterminal with \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{12}\).
Set Up — Add and Subtract \(2\pi\)
\[\dfrac{\pi}{12} + 2\pi \qquad\text{and}\qquad \dfrac{\pi}{12} - 2\pi\]
Rewrite \(2\pi\) with denominator 12 so you can combine the fractions.
Common Denominator
\[2\pi = \dfrac{24\pi}{12}\]
Combine
\[\dfrac{\pi}{12} + \dfrac{24\pi}{12} = \dfrac{25\pi}{12} \qquad \dfrac{\pi}{12} - \dfrac{24\pi}{12} = -\dfrac{23\pi}{12}\]
Two Coterminal Angles
\[\dfrac{25\pi}{12} \quad\text{(positive)} \qquad -\dfrac{23\pi}{12} \quad\text{(negative)}\]
Reference
Standard Angles — Degrees & Radians on the Unit Circle
Every special angle has matched degree and radian labels. Memorizing this picture pays off for every trig problem.
Concept
The Bridge: \(180° = \pi\) Radians
A half-turn around the origin is \(180°\) in degree measure and \(\pi\) in radian measure. Every degree–radian conversion comes from this one equivalence.
A Straight-Line Half-Rotation
Identity
\[180° = \pi \text{ radians}\]
Divide
\[1° = \dfrac{\pi}{180} \text{ rad} \qquad 1 \text{ rad} = \dfrac{180°}{\pi}\]
Why \(\pi\)?
A full circle's circumference is \(2\pi r\). A half-circle arc on a unit circle has length \(\pi\). That length — measured in radii — is the radian measure of the angle.
Reference
Converting Between Radians and Degrees
Multiply by the conversion ratio that cancels the unit you don't want.
Rad → Deg
\[\theta_{\text{rad}} \times \dfrac{180°}{\pi} = \theta_{\text{deg}}\]
Deg → Rad
\[\theta_{\text{deg}} \times \dfrac{\pi}{180°} = \theta_{\text{rad}}\]
Quick Demo
\(\dfrac{\pi}{5} \times \dfrac{180°}{\pi} = \dfrac{180°}{5} = 36°\). The \(\pi\)s cancel.
Memory Hook
If you want degrees in your answer, put \(180°\) on top. If you want radians, put \(\pi\) on top.
Pure Number Test
Radians are dimensionless — angles like "2" with no °-symbol are always in radians.
Example 2
Convert \(130°\) to Radians
Convert \(130°\) to radian measure. Leave your answer in exact form (in terms of \(\pi\)).
Multiply by \(\pi/180°\)
\[\dfrac{130°}{1} \times \dfrac{\pi}{180°}\]
Cancel and Reduce
\[\dfrac{130\,\pi}{180} = \dfrac{13\pi}{18}\]
Divide top and bottom by 10.
Answer
\[130° = \dfrac{13\pi}{18} \text{ rad}\]
Example 3
Convert \(-\dfrac{3\pi}{10}\) to Degrees
Convert \(-\dfrac{3\pi}{10}\) radians to degree measure.
Multiply by \(180°/\pi\)
\[-\dfrac{3\pi}{10} \times \dfrac{180°}{\pi}\]
Cancel the \(\pi\) and Simplify
\[-\dfrac{3 \cdot 180°}{10} = -\dfrac{540°}{10} = -54°\]
Answer
\[-\dfrac{3\pi}{10} = -54°\]
Example 4
Convert \(2\) Radians to Degrees
Convert \(2\) radians to degree measure. The given angle isn't a multiple of \(\pi\), so expect a decimal answer.
Multiply by \(180°/\pi\)
\[2 \times \dfrac{180°}{\pi} = \dfrac{360°}{\pi}\]
The \(\pi\) stays in the denominator because there's no \(\pi\) up top to cancel it.
Evaluate
\[\dfrac{360°}{\pi} \approx \dfrac{360°}{3.14159\ldots} \approx 114.59°\]
Answer
\[2 \text{ rad} \approx 114.59°\]
Concept
Complementary and Supplementary Angles
Two positive angles are complementary when they sum to \(90°\) (or \(\pi/2\)) and supplementary when they sum to \(180°\) (or \(\pi\)).
Complementary — sum is 90°
Supplementary — sum is 180°
Complement of \(\theta\)
In degrees: \(90° - \theta\). In radians: \(\dfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta\). Only defined when the result is positive.
Supplement of \(\theta\)
In degrees: \(180° - \theta\). In radians: \(\pi - \theta\). Only defined when the result is positive.
Example 5
Complement and Supplement of \(148°\)
If possible, find the complement and supplement of \(148°\).
Try the Complement
\[90° - 148° = -58°\]
A complement must be positive. \(148°\) is already bigger than \(90°\), so no complement exists.
Supplement
\[180° - 148° = 32°\]
Final Answer
\[\text{complement: none} \qquad \text{supplement: } 32°\]
Example 6
Complement and Supplement of \(\dfrac{2\pi}{5}\)
If possible, find the complement and supplement of \(\dfrac{2\pi}{5}\) radians.
Complement — Subtract from \(\pi/2\)
\[\dfrac{\pi}{2} - \dfrac{2\pi}{5} = \dfrac{5\pi}{10} - \dfrac{4\pi}{10} = \dfrac{\pi}{10}\]
Common denominator is 10. The result is positive, so the complement exists.
Supplement — Subtract from \(\pi\)
\[\pi - \dfrac{2\pi}{5} = \dfrac{5\pi}{5} - \dfrac{2\pi}{5} = \dfrac{3\pi}{5}\]
Final Answer
\[\text{complement: } \dfrac{\pi}{10} \qquad \text{supplement: } \dfrac{3\pi}{5}\]
Concept
Radians Without the \(\pi\)
In which quadrant is the terminal side of an angle of \(3\) radians?
Anchor with Familiar Landmarks
\[\dfrac{\pi}{2} \approx 1.571 \qquad \pi \approx 3.142 \qquad \dfrac{3\pi}{2} \approx 4.712 \qquad 2\pi \approx 6.283\]
Place \(3\) on the Number Line
\[\dfrac{\pi}{2} \;\;<\;\; 3 \;\;<\;\; \pi\]
So 3 radians lands between the top of the \(y\)-axis and the negative \(x\)-axis.
Quadrant
\[3 \text{ radians lies in } \textbf{Quadrant II.}\]
Wrap-up
By the End of 4.2 You Should Be Able To...
Check yourself against each of these. If anything feels shaky, revisit the relevant example before moving on to 4.3.
Skill 1 — Standard Position
Sketch an angle in standard position and identify the vertex, initial side, and terminal side.
Skill 2 — Direction
Distinguish positive (counterclockwise) from negative (clockwise) rotation when drawing an angle.
Skill 3 — Coterminal Angles
Generate coterminal angles by adding or subtracting \(360^\circ\) (or \(2\pi\)) one full revolution at a time.
Skill 4 — Conversion
Convert between degrees and radians using the bridge \(180^\circ = \pi\), leaving exact answers when possible.
Skill 5 — Complement & Supplement
Find an angle's complement (sum \(90^\circ\) or \(\pi/2\)) and supplement (sum \(180^\circ\) or \(\pi\)), in either unit.
Skill 6 — Recognize Radians
Recognize when an angle is expressed in radians without a \(\pi\) and locate it on the unit circle by comparing to \(\pi/2\) and \(\pi\).
