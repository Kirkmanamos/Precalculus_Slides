8.1 Polar Coordinates
Objective: Plot points using polar coordinates, and convert coordinates and equations between polar and rectangular forms.
pole & polar axis coordinate conversion equation conversion polar grid
Reference
The Polar Coordinate System
  • The system is built on a two-dimensional plane using:
    • The pole — a fixed point representing the ________origin.
    • The polar axis — a fixed ray pointing in the positive \(x\)-direction.
  • A point \(P\) is described by the ordered pair \((r, \theta)\):
    • \(r\) — the __________________directed distance from the pole to \(P\).
    • \(\theta\) — the ________angle from the polar axis to the segment \(OP\).
  • Negative \(r\): If \(r < 0\), move in the ____________opposite direction of \(\theta\). The point \((-r, \theta)\) is equivalent to \((r, \theta + \pi)\).
Multiple Representations
Every point has infinitely many polar coordinates: \[(r, \theta) = (r, \theta + 2\pi k) = (-r, \theta + \pi) \quad \text{for any integer } k\]
Example 1
Plotting Polar Points
Plot the points \(A(2, 60^\circ)\), \(B(3, \frac{\pi}{2})\), \(C(-2, 30^\circ)\), and \(D(1, \frac{7\pi}{4})\) on a polar grid.
Point A (2, 60°)
Rotate \(60^\circ\) CCW, extend 2 units.
Point B (3, \(\frac{\pi}{2}\))
Rotate \(90^\circ\) CCW, extend 3 units. Same as \((0, 3)\).
Point C (-2, 30°)
Rotate \(30^\circ\) CCW, extend 2 units in the opposite direction (lands in QIII).
Point D (1, \(\frac{7\pi}{4}\))
Rotate \(315^\circ\) CCW (or \(45^\circ\) CW), extend 1 unit.

Polar–Rectangular Conversion Formulas
Polar to Rectangular
\(x = \) ___________\(r\cos\theta\)
\(y = \) ___________\(r\sin\theta\)
Rectangular to Polar
\(r^2 = \) ___________\(x^2 + y^2\)
\(\tan\theta = \) ___________\(y/x\) \(\quad (x \ne 0)\)
Finding the Angle \(\theta\)
Use \(\theta = \arctan(y/x)\) to find a reference angle, then adjust for the correct quadrant based on the signs of \(x\) and \(y\).
Examples 2 & 3
Convert Polar to Rectangular Coordinates
Convert the following polar points to rectangular coordinates: (a) \(\left(3, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)\) and (b) \(\left(-2, \frac{\pi}{6}\right)\) OpenStax
Part (a): \(r = 3\) and \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{2}\)
\[x = 3\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 3(0) = 0 \qquad y = 3\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 3(1) = 3\]
Substitute into conversion formulas.
Rectangular: \(\boxed{(0, 3)}\)
Part (b): \(r = -2\) and \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{6}\)
\[x = -2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = -2\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right) = -\sqrt{3} \qquad y = -2\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = -2\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = -1\]
A negative radius points opposite \(\frac{\pi}{6}\) in QIII.
Rectangular: \(\boxed{(-\sqrt{3}, -1)}\)
Examples 4 & 5
Convert Rectangular to Polar Coordinates
Convert the following rectangular points to polar coordinates: (a) \((3, 3)\) and (b) \((-1, \sqrt{3})\) OpenStax
Part (a): \((3,3)\) in QI
\[r = \sqrt{3^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{18} = 3\sqrt{2}\]
\[\tan\theta = \frac{3}{3} = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; \theta = \frac{\pi}{4} \quad \text{(QI)}\]
Find modulus and argument.
Polar: \(\boxed{\left(3\sqrt{2}, \frac{\pi}{4}\right)}\)
Part (b): \((-1, \sqrt{3})\) in QII
\[r = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + (\sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{1+3} = 2\]
\[\tan\theta = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{-1} = -\sqrt{3} \;\Longrightarrow\; \theta = \pi - \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{2\pi}{3} \quad \text{(QII)}\]
Adjust \(\theta\) for Quadrant II.
Polar: \(\boxed{\left(2, \frac{2\pi}{3}\right)}\)
Concept

Converting Equations

Rectangular to Polar
Replace \(x\) with \(r\cos\theta\) and \(y\) with \(r\sin\theta\). Simplify using the Pythagorean identity \(r^2 = x^2 + y^2\) when possible.
Polar to Rectangular
Express the equation in terms of \(r^2\), \(r\cos\theta\), or \(r\sin\theta\). You may need to square both sides, or multiply both sides by \(r\), before substituting \(x\), \(y\), and \(x^2+y^2\).
Goal
The resulting equation should contain ONLY polar variables \((r, \theta)\) or ONLY rectangular variables \((x,y)\). Never mix them in the final answer.
Examples 6, 7 & 8
Convert Equations between Forms
Transform: (a) \(x^2 + y^2 = 9\) to polar, (b) \(r = 2\sec\theta\) to rectangular, and (c) \(r = 3\sin\theta\) to rectangular. OpenStax
Part (a): \(x^2 + y^2 = 9\)
\[\text{Substitute } x^2 + y^2 = r^2: \quad r^2 = 9 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{r = 3}\]
Describes a circle of radius 3 centered at the pole.
Part (b): \(r = 2\sec\theta\)
\[r = \frac{2}{\cos\theta} \;\Longrightarrow\; r\cos\theta = 2 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{x = 2}\]
Describes a vertical line.
Part (c): \(r = 3\sin\theta\)
\[r^2 = 3r\sin\theta \;\Longrightarrow\; x^2 + y^2 = 3y \;\Longrightarrow\; x^2 + y^2 - 3y = 0\]
\[x^2 + \left(y - \frac{3}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{9}{4}\]
Multiply by \(r\), substitute, and complete the square. Circle: center \((0, \frac{3}{2})\), radius \(\frac{3}{2}\).
Reference
Standard Line Forms in Polar Coordinates
Vertical Line
\[x = c \;\Longleftrightarrow\; r = c\sec\theta\]
Horizontal Line
\[y = c \;\Longleftrightarrow\; r = c\csc\theta\]
Line Through Origin
A line passing through the origin at angle \(\alpha\) is written in polar coordinates simply as: \[\theta = \alpha\]
