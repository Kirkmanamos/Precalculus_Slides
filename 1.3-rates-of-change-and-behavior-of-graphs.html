1.3 Rates of Change and Behavior of Graphs
Objective: Calculate the average rate of change of a function, identify intervals of increase, decrease, or constant behavior, and locate local and absolute extrema.
average rate of change secant line increasing & decreasing local extrema absolute extrema
Definition
Average Rate of Change
OpenStax
Average Rate of Change
The average rate of change between two input values is the change in the output values divided by the change in the input values. \[\text{Average Rate of Change} = \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1}\]
Geometric Interpretation
It represents the ________________slope of the secant line passing through the points \((x_1,\, f(x_1))\) and \((x_2,\, f(x_2))\) on the graph.
Units of Measurement
The units are always ________________output units per input unit (e.g., miles per hour, dollars per year).
Constant vs. Variable Rate
For linear functions, the average rate of change is constant (always equal to the slope \(m\)). For non-linear functions, it varies depending on the chosen interval \([x_1,\, x_2]\).
Example 1
Average Rate of Change from a Table
OpenStax
The table shows the average price of a gallon of gasoline in the US between 2005 and 2020. Find the average rate of change of gas prices between the given years.
Year (\(t\))20052008201220162020
Price \(P(t)\) (\$)2.303.273.642.142.60
a) Between 2005 and 2012
\[\dfrac{P(2012) - P(2005)}{2012 - 2005} = \dfrac{3.64 - 2.30}{12 - 5} = \dfrac{1.34}{7} \approx 0.191 \text{ dollars per year}\]
b) Between 2012 and 2020
\[\dfrac{P(2020) - P(2012)}{2020 - 2012} = \dfrac{2.60 - 3.64}{20 - 12} = \dfrac{-1.04}{8} = -0.130 \text{ dollars per year}\]
Interpretation
On average, gas prices rose by about \(19.1\) cents per year from 2005 to 2012, and then fell by \(13.0\) cents per year from 2012 to 2020.
Example 2
Average Rate of Change of a Formula
OpenStax
Find the average rate of change of \(f(x) = x^2 - \dfrac{1}{x}\) on the interval \([2,\, 4]\).
Step 1 · Evaluate at the endpoints
\[f(2) = (2)^2 - \dfrac{1}{2} = 4 - 0.5 = 3.5\] \[f(4) = (4)^2 - \dfrac{1}{4} = 16 - 0.25 = 15.75\]
Step 2 · Apply the formula
\[\text{Average Rate of Change} = \dfrac{f(4) - f(2)}{4 - 2} = \dfrac{15.75 - 3.5}{4 - 2}\]
Step 3 · Simplify
\[= \dfrac{12.25}{2} = 6.125\]
Quick Check
Compute the rate
What is the average rate of change of \(f(x) = x^2\) on \([1,\, 3]\)?
Correct. \(\dfrac{f(3)-f(1)}{3-1} = \dfrac{9-1}{2} = 4\) — the slope of the secant line.
It’s \(\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{f(3)-f(1)}{3-1} = \dfrac{9-1}{2} = 4\). Don’t forget to divide by \(\Delta x\).
Concept
Increasing, Decreasing, or Constant
OpenStax
Increasing
Graph rises from left to right.
Outputs increase as inputs increase:
\(f(x_1) < f(x_2)\) when \(x_1 < x_2\).
Decreasing
Graph falls from left to right.
Outputs decrease as inputs increase:
\(f(x_1) > f(x_2)\) when \(x_1 < x_2\).
Constant
Graph is flat.
Outputs do not change:
\(f(x_1) = f(x_2)\) for all \(x\).
Reporting Intervals
When identifying where a function is increasing, decreasing, or constant, always write the intervals in terms of ________________input (x) values.

Identify Behavior Intervals from Graph

Find the intervals where the graphed function is increasing or decreasing.
Increasing Intervals
Graph rises left-to-right from \(-\infty\) to the peak at \(x = 1\), and from the valley at \(x = 4\) to \(\infty\).

Quick Check
Where is it decreasing?
A graph peaks at \((1,\, 3)\) and bottoms out at \((4,\, -2)\). On which interval is it decreasing?
Correct. The graph falls between the peak and the valley, and intervals use \(x\)-values: \((1,\, 4)\).
Decreasing is where the graph falls — between the peak and the valley. Report it with \(x\)-values, not \(y\): \((1,\, 4)\). (C is where it’s increasing.)
Concept
Local and Absolute Extrema
OpenStax
Local (Relative) Extrema
• Local Maximum: A point that is higher than any nearby points (a hill peak).
• Local Minimum: A point that is lower than any nearby points (a valley bottom).
Absolute (Global) Extrema
• Absolute Maximum: The highest output value over the function's entire domain.
• Absolute Minimum: The lowest output value over the function's entire domain.
Open vs. Closed Domains
On open intervals like \((-\infty,\, \infty)\), graphs heading to \(\pm\infty\) have no absolute extrema. On closed intervals like \([a,\, b]\), absolute extrema must exist and can occur at ________________endpoints. Local extrema cannot occur at endpoints.
Example 4
Identify Extrema from Graph
OpenStax
Using the graph from Example 3, identify all local and absolute extrema on the domain \((-\infty,\, \infty)\).
Local Maximum
Occurs at the peak: \(y = 3\) at \(x = 1\).
Point: \((1,\, 3)\)
Local Minimum
Occurs at the valley: \(y = -2\) at \(x = 4\).
Point: \((4,\, -2)\)
Absolute Extrema
Since the curve continues infinitely to \(\infty\) and \(-\infty\):
• No Absolute Maximum
• No Absolute Minimum
Quick Check
Extrema at an endpoint?
On a closed interval \([a,\, b]\), can a local maximum occur at an endpoint?
Correct. A local extremum needs points on both sides, so it must be interior. Endpoints can still be absolute extrema.
A local max/min needs nearby points on both sides, so it can’t sit at an endpoint. An endpoint can still be the absolute max or min.
Example 5
Extrema on a Closed Interval
OpenStax
Suppose the function is restricted to the closed domain \([0,\, 5]\), where \(f(0) = 1\) and \(f(5) = 1\). Find the local and absolute extrema.
Step 1 · Identify Local Extrema
Local extrema are restricted to interior points:
• Local max of \(3\) at \(x = 1\)
• Local min of \(-2\) at \(x = 4\)
Step 2 · Compare with Endpoints for Absolute Max
Compare interior peak (\(y=3\)) and endpoints (\(f(0)=1\), \(f(5)=1\)).
• Absolute Maximum: \(3\) at \(x = 1\)
Step 3 · Compare with Endpoints for Absolute Min
Compare interior valley (\(y=-2\)) and endpoints (\(f(0)=1\), \(f(5)=1\)).
• Absolute Minimum: \(-2\) at \(x = 4\)
Summary
Big Ideas · 1.3
Average Rate of Change
Calculated as \(\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1}\). Represents secant line slope.
Secant vs Tangent
Secant line cuts through two points on a curve. Tangent line touches at exactly one point (inst. rate of change).
Behavior Intervals
Increasing = rises left-to-right. Decreasing = falls left-to-right. Write intervals using only \(x\)-values.
Local Extrema
Local peaks (maxima) and local valleys (minima) on the interior of the function's domain.
Absolute Extrema
The absolute highest or lowest outputs over the entire domain. Can occur at endpoints.
Endpoints
On a closed interval, endpoints must be checked as potential candidates for absolute maximums and minimums.
