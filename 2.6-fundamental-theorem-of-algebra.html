2.6 Fundamental Theorem of Algebra
Objective: Use the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra to count zeros, factor any polynomial completely over the complex numbers, apply the conjugate-zero theorems to recover missing zeros, and build polynomials with given zeros and an additional condition.

Reference
The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra
A polynomial with degree \(n \geq 1\) is guaranteed to have a complete set of zeros — provided we look in the complex numbers.
  • Every polynomial equation with degree greater than zero has ________exactly \(n\) ________solutions in the set of complex numbers (counting multiplicity).
  • The polynomial therefore factors completely as a product of ________\(n\) linear factors over \(\mathbb{C}\).
consequence
\[f(x) = a\,(x - r_1)(x - r_2)\,\cdots\,(x - r_n)\]
Counting Rule
A degree-\(n\) polynomial has \(n\) zeros in \(\mathbb{C}\). A repeated zero of multiplicity \(k\) counts as \(k\) of those zeros.
Why Complex Matters
Over the real numbers a polynomial can have fewer than \(n\) zeros. Over \(\mathbb{C}\), the count is exact — no zeros are ever missing.
Real Coefficients
If the coefficients are real, any non-real zero must come paired with its complex conjugate. So the complex zeros always appear in pairs.

Conjugate-Zero Theorems
When a polynomial has real coefficients, non-real and irrational zeros never come alone — they come paired with their conjugates.
Complex Conjugate Zeros
If \(a + bi\) is a zero, then ________\(a - bi\) is also a zero.
Irrational Conjugates
If \(a + \sqrt{b}\) is a zero, then ________\(a - \sqrt{b}\) is also a zero.
Counting Pairs
Non-real zeros always pair up. A degree-\(n\) polynomial with real coefficients has an even number of non-real zeros.
Building Real Factors
Multiplying a conjugate pair gives a quadratic with real coefficients: \[(x - (a + bi))(x - (a - bi)) = (x - a)^2 + b^2\]
Real Coefficients Required
Both theorems assume the polynomial has real coefficients. With non-real coefficients, conjugate pairings don't have to hold.
Example 3
Build a Cubic from Zeros \(2,\; \sqrt{3}\) with \(g(4) = 13\)
Write the cubic polynomial \(g\) with real coefficients that has zeros \(2\) and \(\sqrt{3}\) and where \(g(4) = 13\).
Step 1 — Identify the third zero
By the Irrational Conjugates Theorem, since \(\sqrt{3}\) is a zero, so is \(-\sqrt{3}\). Three zeros: \(2,\, \sqrt{3},\, -\sqrt{3}\).
Step 2 — Write the factored form
\[g(x) = a\,(x - 2)(x - \sqrt{3})(x + \sqrt{3})\]
Step 3 — Multiply the conjugate pair
\[(x - \sqrt{3})(x + \sqrt{3}) = x^2 - 3\]
\[g(x) = a\,(x - 2)(x^2 - 3)\]
Step 4 — Use \(g(4) = 13\) to solve for \(a\)
\[g(4) = a(4 - 2)(16 - 3) = a(2)(13) = 26a = 13 \;\Longrightarrow\; a = \tfrac{1}{2}\]
Cubic Polynomial
\[g(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(x - 2)(x^2 - 3) = \tfrac{1}{2}x^3 - x^2 - \tfrac{3}{2}x + 3\]
Example 4
Build a Cubic from Zeros \(2,\; 1 - i\) with \(f(1) = 3\)
Find a cubic polynomial \(f\) with real coefficients that has \(2\) and \(1 - i\) as zeros and \(f(1) = 3\).
Step 1 — Identify the third zero
By the Complex Conjugate Zeros Theorem, since \(1 - i\) is a zero, so is \(1 + i\). Three zeros: \(2,\, 1 - i,\, 1 + i\).
Step 2 — Write the factored form
\[f(x) = a\,(x - 2)\bigl(x - (1 - i)\bigr)\bigl(x - (1 + i)\bigr)\]
Step 3 — Multiply the conjugate pair
\[\bigl((x - 1) + i\bigr)\bigl((x - 1) - i\bigr) = (x - 1)^2 - i^2 = (x - 1)^2 + 1\]
\[= x^2 - 2x + 2\]
Step 4 — Use \(f(1) = 3\) to solve for \(a\)
\[f(x) = a\,(x - 2)(x^2 - 2x + 2)\]
\[f(1) = a\,(1 - 2)(1 - 2 + 2) = a(-1)(1) = -a = 3 \;\Longrightarrow\; a = -3\]
Cubic Polynomial
\[f(x) = -3(x - 2)(x^2 - 2x + 2)\]
Example 5
Given \(1 + 3i\) is a Zero: Find All Zeros of \(f(x) = x^4 - 6x^3 + 47x^2 - 98x + 290\)
A quartic with real coefficients has \(1 + 3i\) as a zero. Find the remaining three zeros.
Step 1 — Use the conjugate pair
By CCZ, \(1 - 3i\) is also a zero. Multiply this conjugate pair to get a real quadratic factor.
\[\bigl((x - 1) - 3i\bigr)\bigl((x - 1) + 3i\bigr) = (x - 1)^2 + 9 = x^2 - 2x + 10\]
Step 2 — Long divide \(f(x)\) by \(x^2 - 2x + 10\)
First term: \(x^4 \div x^2 = x^2\). Multiply back, subtract.
\[(x^4 - 6x^3 + 47x^2) - (x^4 - 2x^3 + 10x^2) = -4x^3 + 37x^2\]
Step 3 — Next term: \(-4x^3 \div x^2 = -4x\)
Multiply: \(-4x(x^2 - 2x + 10) = -4x^3 + 8x^2 - 40x\). Subtract.
\[(-4x^3 + 37x^2 - 98x) - (-4x^3 + 8x^2 - 40x) = 29x^2 - 58x\]
Step 4 — Final term: \(29x^2 \div x^2 = 29\)
Multiply: \(29(x^2 - 2x + 10) = 29x^2 - 58x + 290\). Subtract — remainder is \(0\).
\[\text{Quotient: } x^2 - 4x + 29\]
Step 5 — Solve \(x^2 - 4x + 29 = 0\) with the Quadratic Formula
\[x = \dfrac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 - 116}}{2} = \dfrac{4 \pm \sqrt{-100}}{2} = \dfrac{4 \pm 10i}{2} = 2 \pm 5i\]
All Four Zeros
\[x = 1 + 3i,\quad 1 - 3i,\quad 2 + 5i,\quad 2 - 5i\]
Two complex conjugate pairs — a quartic with real coefficients and four non-real zeros.
Review
Can You Now...?
Use this checklist to confirm you can hunt down every zero of any polynomial.
Count Zeros
A degree-\(n\) polynomial has exactly \(n\) zeros over the complex numbers, counting multiplicity.
Factor Completely
Hunt rational zeros, divide them out, and use the Quadratic Formula on whatever quadratic factor remains.
Use Conjugate Pairs
With real coefficients, \(a + bi\) and \(a + \sqrt{b}\) always pair with their conjugates. One given non-real zero secretly delivers two.
Build Quadratic Factors
Pair the conjugate factors first — the radicals or imaginary parts cancel by difference of squares, leaving a tidy quadratic with real coefficients.
Build From Conditions
Use given zeros to write the factored form. Use a known function value (like \(f(1) = 3\)) to solve for the leading coefficient \(a\).
Divide by a Known Factor
When given a complex zero, multiply the conjugate pair into a real quadratic, then long-divide to reduce the polynomial by two degrees at once.
Over the real numbers, polynomials can hide zeros. Over the complex numbers, every degree-\(n\) polynomial has exactly \(n\) zeros — no exceptions, no missing roots.
