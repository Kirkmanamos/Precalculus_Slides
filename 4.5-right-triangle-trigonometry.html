Unit 4 · Intro to Trigonometry
4.1 Right Triangle Trigonometry
Objective: Evaluate trigonometric functions of acute angles, use fundamental identities, and apply right-triangle trig to model real-life problems.
SOHCAHTOA six ratios reciprocal & cofunction identities degrees / minutes / seconds elevation & depression
Concept
SOHCAHTOA — The Three Primary Ratios
For an acute angle \(\theta\) in a right triangle, the three primary trigonometric ratios compare two side lengths each.
Reference Right Triangle
S O H
\(\sin\theta = \dfrac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\)
C A H
\(\cos\theta = \dfrac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\)
T O A
\(\tan\theta = \dfrac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}\)
Reading the Triangle
"Opposite" and "adjacent" are always relative to the angle you're working with. The hypotenuse is always the side across from the right angle.
Reference
The Six Trigonometric Functions
Three primary ratios — and three reciprocals. The reciprocal pairs are (sin, csc), (cos, sec), and (tan, cot).
sin / csc
\[\sin\theta = \dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}} \qquad \csc\theta = \dfrac{\text{hyp}}{\text{opp}} = \dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\]
cos / sec
\[\cos\theta = \dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}} \qquad \sec\theta = \dfrac{\text{hyp}}{\text{adj}} = \dfrac{1}{\cos\theta}\]
tan / cot
\[\tan\theta = \dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}} \qquad \cot\theta = \dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{opp}} = \dfrac{1}{\tan\theta}\]
Reciprocal Pairs
Sine ↔ Cosecant, Cosine ↔ Secant, Tangent ↔ Cotangent. Flipping the ratio (\(\text{opp}/\text{hyp} \to \text{hyp}/\text{opp}\)) gives the reciprocal function. The "co" in cosine, cotangent, and cosecant comes from complementary — those names mark cofunction pairs, which we'll meet later.
Example 1
Find the Six Trig Functions for Angle \(G\)
In right triangle \(\triangle ABG\), the right angle is at \(B\), with \(AB = 21\), \(BG = 28\), and \(AG = 35\). Find the six trig functions of angle \(G\).
Locate the Sides Relative to \(G\)
From angle \(G\):
  • opposite \(= AB = 21\)
  • adjacent \(= BG = 28\)
  • hypotenuse \(= AG = 35\)
Check: \(21^2 + 28^2 = 441 + 784 = 1225 = 35^2\) ✓
Primary Ratios — Reduce
\[\sin G = \dfrac{21}{35} = \dfrac{3}{5} \qquad \cos G = \dfrac{28}{35} = \dfrac{4}{5} \qquad \tan G = \dfrac{21}{28} = \dfrac{3}{4}\]
Reciprocal Ratios — Flip
\[\csc G = \dfrac{5}{3} \qquad \sec G = \dfrac{5}{4} \qquad \cot G = \dfrac{4}{3}\]
All Six Trig Functions
\(\sin G = \dfrac{3}{5}\)
\(\csc G = \dfrac{5}{3}\)
\(\cos G = \dfrac{4}{5}\)
\(\sec G = \dfrac{5}{4}\)
\(\tan G = \dfrac{3}{4}\)
\(\cot G = \dfrac{4}{3}\)
Example 2
Given One Ratio, Find the Rest
Given \(\cos\theta = \dfrac{7}{8}\), find the other five trig ratios of \(\theta\) (assume \(\theta\) is acute).
Set Up: Cos = adj / hyp
\[\cos\theta = \dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}} = \dfrac{7}{8} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \text{adj} = 7,\;\; \text{hyp} = 8\]
Pick a right triangle whose adjacent side is 7 and hypotenuse is 8. Then find the missing leg.
Find the Opposite Side (Pythagorean Theorem)
\(\text{opp}^2 + 7^2 = 8^2\)
\(\text{opp}^2 = 64 - 49 = 15\)
\(\text{opp} = \sqrt{15}\)
Build the Remaining Five Ratios
\[\sin\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{15}}{8} \qquad \tan\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{15}}{7}\]
\[\csc\theta = \dfrac{8}{\sqrt{15}} = \dfrac{8\sqrt{15}}{15} \qquad \sec\theta = \dfrac{8}{7} \qquad \cot\theta = \dfrac{7}{\sqrt{15}} = \dfrac{7\sqrt{15}}{15}\]
Final Answer (Rationalized)
\(\sin\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{15}}{8}\)
\(\csc\theta = \dfrac{8\sqrt{15}}{15}\)
\(\cos\theta = \dfrac{7}{8}\)
\(\sec\theta = \dfrac{8}{7}\)
\(\tan\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{15}}{7}\)
\(\cot\theta = \dfrac{7\sqrt{15}}{15}\)
Concept
Building 30-60-90 from an Equilateral Triangle
An equilateral triangle, split down the middle by its altitude, produces a 30-60-90 right triangle with exact side ratios \(1 : \sqrt{3} : 2\).
Equilateral Triangle of Side 2, Bisected
Step 1 — Use Equilateral Property
All three sides equal 2, so \(DB = 2\). The altitude \(AC\) bisects \(DB\), giving \(CB = 1\).
Step 2 — Pythagorean Theorem
\[AC^2 + 1^2 = 2^2 \;\Longrightarrow\; AC = \sqrt{3}\]
Result — 30-60-90 Side Ratios
\[1 \;:\; \sqrt{3} \;:\; 2\]
Example 3
Exact Values: \(\sin 60°,\; \cos 60°,\; \sin 30°,\; \cos 30°\)
Use the 30-60-90 triangle (sides \(1, \sqrt{3}, 2\)) from the equilateral triangle to find \(\sin 60°,\; \cos 60°,\; \sin 30°,\; \cos 30°\).
From the Triangle, Identify Sides for Each Angle
For the 60° angle (at \(B\)): opposite \(= \sqrt{3}\), adjacent \(= 1\), hypotenuse \(= 2\).
For the 30° angle (at \(A\)): opposite \(= 1\), adjacent \(= \sqrt{3}\), hypotenuse \(= 2\).
Apply SOHCAHTOA
\[\sin 60° = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \qquad \cos 60° = \dfrac{1}{2}\]
\[\sin 30° = \dfrac{1}{2} \qquad \cos 30° = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\]
Observation — These Values Are Linked
\[\sin 60° = \cos 30° \qquad \cos 60° = \sin 30°\]
Notice 60° and 30° are complementary (they sum to 90°). This pattern is the cofunction identity — coming up next.
Concept
Cofunction Identities
Cofunctions of complementary angles are equal. If two acute angles sum to 90°, swapping a trig function with its "co-" counterpart preserves the value.
sin ↔ cos
\[\sin\theta = \cos(90° - \theta) \qquad \cos\theta = \sin(90° - \theta)\]
tan ↔ cot
\[\tan\theta = \cot(90° - \theta) \qquad \cot\theta = \tan(90° - \theta)\]
sec ↔ csc
\[\sec\theta = \csc(90° - \theta) \qquad \csc\theta = \sec(90° - \theta)\]
Why "co-"?
The "co" in cosine, cotangent, and cosecant literally stands for complementary. Sine and cosine are cofunctions because they trade roles when angles are complementary.
Quick Check
\(\sin 60° = \cos 30°\) because \(60° + 30° = 90°\). Same with \(\tan 20° = \cot 70°\).
Reference
Fundamental Trig Identities
Four families of identities you will use throughout Unit 4 and Unit 5. All are valid for any acute angle \(\theta\).
Reciprocal Identities
\(\sin\theta = \dfrac{1}{\csc\theta}\)    \(\csc\theta = \dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\)
\(\cos\theta = \dfrac{1}{\sec\theta}\)    \(\sec\theta = \dfrac{1}{\cos\theta}\)
\(\tan\theta = \dfrac{1}{\cot\theta}\)    \(\cot\theta = \dfrac{1}{\tan\theta}\)
Quotient Identities
\(\tan\theta = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\)
\(\cot\theta = \dfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\)
Pythagorean Identities
\(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1\)
\(1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta\)
\(1 + \cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta\)
Cofunction Identities
\(\sin\theta = \cos(90°-\theta)\)
\(\cos\theta = \sin(90°-\theta)\)
\(\tan\theta = \cot(90°-\theta)\)
\(\cot\theta = \tan(90°-\theta)\)
\(\sec\theta = \csc(90°-\theta)\)
\(\csc\theta = \sec(90°-\theta)\)
Power Notation
\(\sin^2\theta\) means \((\sin\theta)^2\), not \(\sin(\theta^2)\). The same convention applies to all six trig functions. Reciprocal identities work with powers too: \(\sin^2\theta = \dfrac{1}{\csc^2\theta}\).
Example 4
Two Methods: Using Triangles vs. Using Identities
Let \(\theta\) be an acute angle with \(\tan\theta = \dfrac{1}{3}\). Find (a) \(\cot\theta\) and (b) \(\sec\theta\).
Method 1 — Build a Reference Triangle
\(\tan\theta = \dfrac{1}{3}\;\Rightarrow\;\text{opp}=1,\;\text{adj}=3\)
\(\text{hyp} = \sqrt{1^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{10}\)
From the triangle:
\(\cot\theta = \dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{opp}} = \dfrac{3}{1} = 3\)
\(\sec\theta = \dfrac{\text{hyp}}{\text{adj}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{10}}{3}\)
Method 2 — Use Identities Directly
(a) Reciprocal Identity
\(\cot\theta = \dfrac{1}{\tan\theta} = \dfrac{1}{1/3} = 3\)
(b) Pythagorean Identity
\(\sec^2\theta = 1 + \tan^2\theta = 1 + \left(\dfrac{1}{3}\right)^2 = \dfrac{10}{9}\)
\(\sec\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{10}}{3}\)
Final Answers — Both Methods Agree
\[\cot\theta = 3 \qquad \sec\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{10}}{3}\]
Concept
Degrees, Minutes, and Seconds (DMS)
Like an hour on a clock, a degree can be subdivided into smaller pieces: minutes (\('\)) and seconds (\(''\)).
Degree
\(1°\) = full unit
Minute
\(1° = 60'\)
Second
\(1' = 60''\) so \(1° = 3600''\)
Read an Angle
The angle \(40°\,20'\,50''\) reads as
40 degrees, 20 minutes, 50 seconds.
Convert to Decimal Degrees
\[40 + 20\!\left(\tfrac{1}{60}\right) + 50\!\left(\tfrac{1}{60^{2}}\right) \approx 40.347°\]
Don't Confuse with Time
An hour and a degree both use "minutes" and "seconds," but they're unrelated. A minute of angle has nothing to do with the clock on the wall.
Example 5
Evaluate \(\sec(9°\,40'\,12'')\) with a Calculator
Use a calculator to evaluate \(\sec(9°\,40'\,12'')\). Round to four decimal places.
Step 1 — Convert DMS to Decimal Degrees
\[9°\,40'\,12'' = 9° + \left(\dfrac{40}{60}\right)° + \left(\dfrac{12}{60^2}\right)° \approx 9.67°\]
Step 2 — Evaluate Using \(\sec\theta = 1/\cos\theta\)
\[\sec(9.67°) = \dfrac{1}{\cos(9.67°)} \approx 1.0144\]
Make sure your calculator is set to degree mode, not radian mode.
Final Answer
\[\sec(9°\,40'\,12'') \approx 1.0144\]
Alternatively, most graphing calculators accept DMS input directly using the °, \(\,'\,\), \(\,''\,\) keys.
Concept
Angles of Elevation and Depression
In modeling problems, angles are measured from a horizontal line of sight. Looking up gives an angle of elevation; looking down gives an angle of depression.
Looking Up vs. Looking Down (from the Observer)
Angle of Elevation
Measured up from the horizontal, toward an object above the observer's eye level.
Angle of Depression
Measured down from the horizontal, toward an object below the observer's eye level.
Key Insight
Both angles are measured from a horizontal line — never from a vertical wall or ground.
Example 6
Angle of Depression — Spotting a Toy
Daniel's mom is sitting in a chair. Her eye level is \(3.5\) feet above the floor. She spots a red pen on the floor and measures the angle of depression to be \(65°\). How far is the pen from the chair?
Step 1 — Draw and Label the Right Triangle
From the eye, the angle down to the pen is \(65°\).
The vertical leg is the eye height: \(3.5\) ft.
The horizontal leg \(x\) is the unknown — distance along the floor.
Step 2 — Choose a Ratio That Uses Opposite and Adjacent
\[\tan 65° = \dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}} = \dfrac{3.5}{x} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x = \dfrac{3.5}{\tan 65°}\]
Opposite (3.5) is across from 65°; adjacent (\(x\)) is along the floor. That's tan.
Step 3 — Evaluate
\[x = \dfrac{3.5}{\tan 65°} \approx 1.632 \text{ feet}\]
Example 7
Angle of Elevation — Height of a Smokestack
From a point \(200\) ft from the base of a building, the angle of elevation to the bottom of a smokestack on the roof is \(35°\); to the top of the smokestack it is \(53°\). Find the height of the smokestack alone.
Step 1 — Build Two Right Triangles That Share the Same Base
Let \(x\) = height of building (bottom of stack).
Let \(y\) = height of smokestack alone (above building).
Both triangles share the same 200-ft base.
Then \(x + y\) is the total height from ground to top of stack.
Step 2 — Find the Building Height \(x\) (Lower Triangle)
\[\tan 35° = \dfrac{x}{200} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x = 200\tan 35° \approx 140.042 \text{ ft}\]
Step 3 — Find Total Height \(x + y\) (Upper Triangle)
\[\tan 53° = \dfrac{x + y}{200} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x + y = 200\tan 53° \approx 265.409 \text{ ft}\]
Step 4 — Subtract to Isolate the Smokestack
\[y = (x+y) - x \approx 265.409 - 140.042 \approx 125.367 \text{ ft}\]
