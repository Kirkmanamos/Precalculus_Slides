6.7 Conditional Probability
Objective: Use tables, Venn diagrams, and tree diagrams to compute probabilities under given conditions.
given thattwo-way tablesVenn diagramstree diagramsrestricted sample space
Reference
Conditional Probability
definition
\[P(A\mid B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}\]
reading
\[\text{Read }P(A\mid B)\text{ as 'the probability of }A\text{ given }B'}\]
  • Conditional Probability represents how the ________probability of one event changes when we know another event ________has occurred.
Backward Probability
Conditional probability answers questions like: If we know something already happened, how does that change the chance of another event?
Intersection First
Find the overlap for the numerator, then divide by the restricted total.
Concept
What "Given" Does to the Sample Space
The phrase given that tells you to zoom in on a smaller sample space before you count anything.
Step 1: Restrict
Keep only the outcomes that satisfy the given condition \(B\). Everything outside \(B\) is ignored.
Step 2: Count the Overlap
Inside that restricted sample space, count how many outcomes also satisfy event \(A\).
Step 3: Form the Ratio
That is why \(P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}\): overlap over restricted total.
Student Translation
\[P(\text{bus}\mid \text{9th}) = \dfrac{\text{9th graders who ride the bus}}{\text{all 9th graders}}\]
Example 1
Conditional Probability from a Two-Way Table
A survey of \(300\) students recorded transportation and grade level. Find (a) the probability that a student is a 12th grader who drives a car and (b) the probability that a student rides the bus, given that the student is in 9th grade.
Read the Table

Joint Probability
\[P(\text{12th and Car}) = \dfrac{45}{300} = \dfrac{3}{20}\]
Conditional Probability
\[P(\text{Bus}\mid \text{9th}) = \dfrac{36}{76} = \dfrac{9}{19}\]
Key Insight
For \(P(A\mid B)\), the denominator is always the total of the given row or column — not the grand total. The "given" condition restricts your sample space.

Theme Park Survey
People exiting a new ride were asked whether they liked or disliked the ride. Find (a) \(P(\text{adult or disliked})\) and (b) \(P(\text{adult}\mid\text{disliked})\).

Example 3
Conditional Probability from a Venn Diagram
In a class of \(25\) students, \(14\) use Instagram and \(16\) use TikTok. One student uses neither platform and six students use both. Find the probability that a randomly selected student uses Instagram given that the student uses TikTok.
Build the Venn Diagram
Venn Diagram
TikTok users are the restricted sample space, so we look inside the TikTok circle only.
Restrict to TikTok Users
\[P(\text{Instagram}\mid \text{TikTok}) = \dfrac{6}{16}\]
Answer
\[P(\text{Instagram}\mid \text{TikTok}) = \dfrac{3}{8}\]
Example 4
Fruit Preferences
In a class of \(40\) students, \(34\) like bananas, \(22\) like pineapples, and \(2\) dislike both fruits. Find (a) the probability that a student likes bananas given that the student likes pineapples and (b) the probability that a student dislikes pineapples given that the student likes bananas.
Find the Overlap First
Venn Diagram
Since 2 students like neither, 38 students like at least one fruit. The overlap is \(34 + 22 - 38 = 18\).
Part (a): Bananas Given Pineapples
\[P(B\mid P) = \dfrac{18}{22} = \dfrac{9}{11}\]
Part (b): Not Pineapples Given Bananas
\[P(P'\mid B) = \dfrac{16}{34} = \dfrac{8}{17}\]
Example 5
Conditional Probability from a Tree Diagram
Emma studies in her bedroom \(40\%\) of the time and at the library \(60\%\) of the time. In her bedroom she does math \(70\%\) of the time; at the library she does math \(50\%\) of the time. If Emma is doing math, what is the probability she is in her bedroom?
Draw the Tree
Emma's Study Tree
Emma studies in her bedroom 40 percent of the time and at the library 60 percent of the time. We compare the joint probabilities that end in Math.
Find the Math Outcomes
\[P(\text{Bedroom and Math}) = 0.4(0.7) = 0.28\]
\[P(\text{Math}) = 0.28 + 0.30 = 0.58\]
Form the Conditional Probability
\[P(\text{Bedroom}\mid \text{Math}) = \dfrac{0.28}{0.58} = \dfrac{14}{29}\]
Your Turn
Tree Diagram Practice
A factory has two machines. Machine A produces \(60\%\) of all items and Machine B produces \(40\%\). Machine A has a \(5\%\) defect rate and Machine B has a \(3\%\) defect rate. If an item is found to be defective, what is the probability it came from Machine A?
Hint
Compute the two defective branch probabilities first. Then divide the Machine A defective branch by the total defective probability.
Answer
\[P(A \cap D) = 0.60 \times 0.05 = 0.03, \quad P(B \cap D) = 0.40 \times 0.03 = 0.012\]
\[P(D) = 0.03 + 0.012 = 0.042\]
\[P(A\mid D) = \dfrac{0.03}{0.042} = \dfrac{5}{7} \approx 0.714\]
Example 6
Weighted Basket Choice
Basket A contains \(3\) red and \(2\) white tickets. Basket B contains \(4\) red and \(1\) white ticket. A die with four faces marked A and two faces marked B chooses the basket. Given that the selected ticket is red, find the probability that it came from Basket B.
Draw the Weighted Tree
Basket Selection Tree
A weighted die chooses Basket A or Basket B before a ticket is drawn.
Find the Red Outcomes
\[P(B \cap R) = \dfrac{2}{6}\cdot\dfrac{4}{5} = \dfrac{4}{15}\]
\[P(R) = \dfrac{4}{6}\cdot\dfrac{3}{5} + \dfrac{2}{6}\cdot\dfrac{4}{5} = \dfrac{2}{5} + \dfrac{4}{15} = \dfrac{2}{3}\]
Form the Conditional Probability
\[P(B\mid R) = \dfrac{\frac{4}{15}}{\frac{2}{3}} = \dfrac{2}{5}\]
Review
Can You Now...?
Use this checklist to make sure you are restricting the sample space correctly before you calculate.
Read "Given" Correctly
Translate \(P(A\mid B)\) as "the probability of \(A\), knowing \(B\) already happened."
Restrict the Denominator
Use only the outcomes inside the given event for the denominator.
Find the Overlap
Use the intersection \(A \cap B\) for the numerator when both conditions must be true.
Use the Right Representation
Read the data from a table, Venn diagram, or tree diagram without changing the conditional idea.
Compare Branches Carefully
In tree problems, compare the joint outcomes that match the given condition before dividing.
Explain the Restriction
Say out loud what the new sample space is. If you can name it, you can usually compute it.
Most conditional-probability errors happen because the denominator never got restricted.
