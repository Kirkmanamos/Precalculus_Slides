6.6 Probability
Objective: Classify events, use the addition and multiplication rules, solve counting-based probability problems, and use complements strategically.

Concept
Probability Vocabulary
  • An ________experiment is any process of observation that leads to a single, well-defined outcome.
  • The ________sample space (\(S\)) is the set of all possible outcomes of an experiment.
  • An ________event (\(E\)) is any subset of the sample space — a collection of one or more outcomes.
  • ________Probability is a measure of how likely an event is to occur.
Experiment
An experiment is any process that leads to a well-defined outcome, such as rolling a die or drawing a card.
Sample Space
The sample space \(S\) is the set of all possible outcomes.
Event
An event \(E\) is any subset of the sample space.

Basic Probability and Experimental Probability

  • If \(P(E)=0\), then \(E\) is an ________impossible event.
  • If \(P(E)=1\), then \(E\) is a ________certain event.

Probability Scale
Probability Forms
Probabilities can be written as fractions, decimals, or percents.
Impossible vs. Certain
If \(P(E)=0\), the event is impossible. If \(P(E)=1\), the event is certain.
Theoretical vs. Experimental
Theoretical probability is based on reasoning. Experimental probability is based on actual trials.
Example
A fair die has theoretical probability \(\dfrac{1}{6}\) of landing on \(3\). If a coin lands heads \(47\) times in \(100\) flips, the experimental probability is \(\dfrac{47}{100}\).
Concept
Which Probability Tool Fits?
Many probability mistakes happen before the arithmetic starts. First decide what relationship the problem is describing.
Addition Rule
Look for or. Ask whether the events can happen at the same time.
Multiplication Rule
Look for and or repeated events. Ask whether the first outcome changes the second.
Counting-Based Probability
If the problem is about selecting or arranging outcomes, use \(\dfrac{\text{favorable}}{\text{total}}\) with permutations or combinations.
Complement
If the question says at least one, not, or direct counting is messy, think about subtracting from \(1\).
Self-check: am I adding events, multiplying events, counting outcomes, or using a complement?

Addition Rule Examples
Mutually Exclusive

In a class of \(30\) students, \(18\) were born in the United States and \(3\) were born in India.

\[P(\text{U.S. or India}) = \dfrac{18+3}{30} = \dfrac{7}{10}\]

\[P(\text{Chemistry or History}) = \dfrac{20+19-15}{27} = \dfrac{24}{27} = \dfrac{8}{9}\]

Your Turn
Practice: Addition Rule
Classify each pair of events, then find the probability: (a) rolling a \(2\) or a \(5\) on one die, (b) rolling a \(2\) or an even number on one die.
Hint
  • Can "rolling a 2" and "rolling a 5" happen at the same time?
  • Can "rolling a 2" and "rolling an even number" happen at the same time?
  • If there is overlap, subtract it once.
Answer
  • \(P(2 \text{ or } 5) = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{3}\) because the events are mutually exclusive.
  • \(P(2 \text{ or even}) = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{3}{6} - \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{2}\) because the events are inclusive.

Concept
Independent vs. Dependent Events
The word and does not automatically tell you which multiplication rule to use. The real question is whether the first event changes the sample space for the second.
Independent
One outcome does not affect the next. Example: flipping a coin twice or drawing with replacement.
Dependent
One outcome does affect the next. Example: drawing without replacement.
Decision Question
After the first event happens, do the probabilities for the second event stay the same?
Quick Shortcut
With replacement usually means independent. Without replacement usually means dependent.
If the sample space changes, the second probability must change too.
Example 1
Independent vs. Dependent Events
There are \(9\) brown boxes and \(6\) blue boxes on a shelf. Find the probability that Ana and Sebastian both choose brown if (a) Ana replaces her box and (b) Ana keeps her box.
With Replacement
\[P(\text{both brown}) = \dfrac{9}{15}\cdot\dfrac{9}{15} = \dfrac{9}{25}\]
Without Replacement
\[P(\text{both brown}) = \dfrac{9}{15}\cdot\dfrac{8}{14} = \dfrac{12}{35}\]
Why the Answers Differ
  • Replacing keeps the second draw independent.
  • Not replacing changes the sample space for the second draw.
Example 2
Independent Events - Repeated Trials
In a recent survey, 85% of adults said they could not go a full day without their smartphone. If 6 adults are chosen at random, what is the probability that all 6 say they could not go a full day without their smartphone?
Independent Events - Multiply
\[P(\text{all 6}) = (0.85)^6\]
Each adult's response is independent of the others.
Answer
\[P(\text{all 6}) \approx 0.3771\]
Interpret
Even though each individual has an 85% probability, the chance that all 6 agree is only about 38%. Repeated independent events compound quickly.
Your Turn
Practice: Independent Events
A bag has \(5\) red and \(3\) blue marbles. You draw two marbles with replacement. What is the probability both are red?
Hint
Because the marble is replaced, the second draw has the same probabilities as the first. Multiply the two red-draw probabilities.
Answer
\[P(\text{both red}) = \frac{5}{8} \cdot \frac{5}{8} = \frac{25}{64}\]
Concept
Probability with Permutations and Combinations
When outcomes are equally likely, use \(\displaystyle P(E) = \frac{\text{favorable outcomes}}{\text{total outcomes}}\). The main challenge is counting both parts correctly.
Order Matters?
Use permutations when different orders count as different outcomes. Use combinations when order does not matter.
Exactly 2 Red Marbles

From \(8\) red and \(6\) blue marbles, choose \(4\).

\[\dfrac{\binom{8}{2}\binom{6}{2}}{\binom{14}{4}} = \dfrac{60}{143}\]
No Repeated Digits in a 4-Digit PIN

Any of the \(10\) digits may appear in each spot.

\[\dfrac{{}_{10}P_4}{10^4} = \dfrac{10\cdot9\cdot8\cdot7}{10000} = \dfrac{63}{125}\]
Odd First Digit

A 4-digit PIN uses digits \(1\)-\(9\) without repetition.

\[\dfrac{5\cdot8\cdot7\cdot6}{{}_9P_4} = \dfrac{5}{9}\]
Ask first: am I counting arrangements, or just selections?
Your Turn
Practice: Counting-Based Probability
A jar contains \(5\) red marbles and \(4\) blue marbles. If \(3\) marbles are chosen at random, what is the probability that exactly \(2\) are red?
Hint
Order does not matter, so count both the favorable outcomes and the total outcomes with combinations.
Answer
\[P(\text{exactly 2 red}) = \dfrac{\binom{5}{2}\binom{4}{1}}{\binom{9}{3}} = \dfrac{40}{84} = \dfrac{10}{21}\]
Reference
Complement Rule
complement
\[P(E') = 1 - P(E)\]
  • The ________complement of an event \(E\) is the set of all outcomes in the sample space that are not in \(E\).
  • If \(E\) is an event, then ________\(E'\) is the complementary event.
At Least One Yellow

A jar has \(8\) blue and \(4\) yellow marbles. Choose \(3\).

\[1 - \dfrac{\binom{8}{3}}{\binom{12}{3}} = 1 - \dfrac{56}{220} = \dfrac{41}{55}\]
Two Dice, Sum Greater Than 3

The complement is sum \(\le 3\): \((1,1), (1,2), (2,1)\).

\[1 - \dfrac{3}{36} = \dfrac{11}{12}\]
Your Turn
Practice: Complement Rule
A jar contains \(5\) blue marbles and \(3\) yellow marbles. If \(2\) marbles are chosen, what is the probability that at least one is yellow?
Hint
"At least one yellow" is easier to find by subtracting the probability of the complement event "both marbles are blue."
Answer
\[P(\text{at least one yellow}) = 1 - \dfrac{\binom{5}{2}}{\binom{8}{2}} = 1 - \dfrac{10}{28} = \dfrac{9}{14}\]
Review
Can You Now...?
Use this checklist to decide whether you are choosing the right probability tool before you start calculating.
Define the Situation
Identify the experiment, sample space, event, and whether the question is asking for theoretical or experimental probability.
Choose Addition
Recognize when or means an addition rule problem and decide whether there is overlap.
Choose Multiplication
Recognize when and means a multiplication rule problem and decide whether the events are independent or dependent.
Count Outcomes
Use favorable over total, with permutations or combinations, when outcomes come from arrangements or selections.
Use a Complement
Recognize when "at least one" or a difficult direct count should become \(1 - P(\text{complement})\).
Explain the Rule Choice
Say why a problem is addition, multiplication, counting-based, or complement before writing the formula.
If an answer is wrong, check the rule choice first. In probability, that is where most errors start.
