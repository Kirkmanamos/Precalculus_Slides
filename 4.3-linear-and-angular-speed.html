4.3 Linear and Angular Speed
Objective: Compute arc length and sector area, and use them to distinguish between linear and angular speed in circular motion.
arc length sector area angular speed (rad/time) linear speed (length/time) unit conversions
Concept
What Is a Radian, Really?
A radian is defined by a proportion: the share of a full circle's arc taken up by the angle equals the share of \(2\pi\) taken up by the angle's measure.
Central Angle and Its Arc
Proportion
\[\dfrac{\text{arc length}}{\text{circumference}} = \dfrac{\text{central angle}}{2\pi}\]
In Symbols
\[\dfrac{s}{2\pi r} = \dfrac{\theta}{2\pi} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \dfrac{s}{r} = \theta\]
Definition
A radian is the measure of a central angle whose intercepted arc has length equal to the radius. We use the letter \(s\) for arc length.
Concept
One Radian, Two Radians — Just Count Radii Along the Arc
If you wrap a length of one radius along the circle's edge, the angle it cuts out is exactly one radian. Wrap two radii, you get two radians.
arc = r → 1 radian
arc = 2r → 2 radians
Same Ratio, Any Circle
\(\dfrac{\text{arc}}{\text{radius}}\) is unitless — it doesn't matter whether the circle is the size of a coin or the size of the Earth.
Why Radians Beat Degrees
Radians come straight from the geometry of the circle. Degrees are an arbitrary choice (why 360?). For arc-length and speed formulas, radians keep the algebra clean.
Reference
Arc Length Formula
Multiply the radius by the central angle (in radians) to get the arc length.
Arc Length \(s\)
Arc Length
\[s = \theta \, r\]
Use Radians, Not Degrees
\(\theta\) must be in radians. If you're given degrees, convert before plugging in.
Unit Circle Bonus
When \(r = 1\), the formula collapses to \(s = \theta\). On a unit circle, the radian measure of the angle equals the arc length itself.
Example 1
Arc Length for a Reflex Angle
A circle has radius \(r = 4\) inches. Find the length of the arc intercepted by a central angle of \(240°\).
Step 1 — Convert the Angle to Radians
\[240° \times \dfrac{\pi}{180°} = \dfrac{240\pi}{180} = \dfrac{4\pi}{3} \text{ rad}\]
The arc-length formula \(s = \theta r\) requires radians.
Step 2 — Apply the Formula
\[s = \theta r = \left(\dfrac{4\pi}{3}\right)(4)\]
Step 3 — Simplify
\[s = \dfrac{16\pi}{3} \text{ in}\]
Final Answer
\[s = \dfrac{16\pi}{3} \approx 16.76 \text{ in}\]
Reference
Area of a Sector
A sector is the "pie-slice" region between two radii and an arc. Its area is half the angle times the radius squared.
Sector — Pie-Slice Region
Sector Area
\[A = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\theta\, r^{2}\]
Proportion
\[\dfrac{A_{\text{sector}}}{A_{\text{circle}}} = \dfrac{\theta}{2\pi}\]
Same Idea as Arc Length
Both \(s\) and \(A\) come from the same proportion: the slice's share of the angle equals its share of the whole. Once again, \(\theta\) must be in radians.
Concept
Two Ways to Measure "How Fast"
When a point travels around a circle, we can measure how fast the angle changes or how far the point moves. These are different measures with different units.
Angular Speed (\(\omega\))
How fast the central angle changes.
Units: radians per second / minute / hour (or revolutions per minute, etc.)
Linear Speed (\(v\))
How fast a point on the circle moves along the arc.
Units: length per time (cm/sec, ft/min, mph, …)
Connecting Them
Both come from the same revolutions-per-time, then converted: angular uses \(2\pi\) rad/rev, linear uses the circumference per rev.
Reference
Linear and Angular Speed — Setup
Start with rotations per unit time. Multiply by the right conversion factor to land on the unit you want.
Angular
\[\omega = \dfrac{\text{revolutions}}{\text{time}} \times \dfrac{2\pi \text{ rad}}{1 \text{ rev}} = \dfrac{\text{radians}}{\text{time}}\]
Linear
\[v = \dfrac{\text{revolutions}}{\text{time}} \times \dfrac{\text{circumference}}{1 \text{ rev}} = \dfrac{\text{length}}{\text{time}}\]
Pick Your Conversion Factor
Each revolution sweeps \(2\pi\) radians (for angular speed) or travels one circumference \(2\pi r\) (for linear speed). That's the only thing that changes.
Why \(v = r\omega\)
Multiplying angular speed by the radius gives linear speed: every radian along the angle corresponds to \(r\) units along the arc.
Example 2
Linear Speed of a Clock's Second Hand
The second hand of a clock is \(10.2\) cm long. Find the linear speed (in cm/sec) of its tip.
Step 1 — Find the Rate in Revolutions per Second
\[\dfrac{1 \text{ rev}}{60 \text{ sec}}\]
A second hand completes one full revolution every 60 seconds.
Step 2 — Multiply by the Circumference
\[v = \dfrac{1 \text{ rev}}{60 \text{ sec}} \times \dfrac{2\pi(10.2) \text{ cm}}{1 \text{ rev}}\]
Step 3 — Simplify
\[v = \dfrac{20.4\pi}{60} = \dfrac{0.34\pi \text{ cm}}{1 \text{ sec}}\]
Final Answer
\[v \approx 1.068 \text{ cm/sec}\]
If the question asked for cm/min, you'd use the rate \(1 \text{ rev}/1 \text{ min}\) and get \(20.4\pi \approx 64.1\) cm/min.
Example 3
Wind Turbine — Angular and Linear Speed
The blades of a wind turbine are \(116\) ft long. The propeller rotates at \(15\) revolutions per minute. Find (a) the angular speed in rad/min and (b) the linear speed of the tip of a blade.
Part (a) — Set Up Angular Speed
\[\omega = \dfrac{15 \text{ rev}}{1 \text{ min}} \times \dfrac{2\pi \text{ rad}}{1 \text{ rev}}\]
The "rev" units cancel, leaving radians per minute.
Part (a) — Evaluate
\[\omega = 30\pi \approx 94.25 \text{ rad/min}\]
Part (b) — Set Up Linear Speed
\[v = \dfrac{15 \text{ rev}}{1 \text{ min}} \times \dfrac{2\pi(116) \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ rev}}\]
Now we use the circumference \(2\pi r = 232\pi\) feet per revolution.
Part (b) — Evaluate
\[v = 3480\pi \approx 10{,}932.7 \text{ ft/min}\]
Example 4
Wheel Speed in Miles per Hour
A wheel with diameter \(24\) inches rotates at \(800\) rpm. Find (a) the angular speed and (b) the linear speed of a point \(P\) on the circumference, in mi/hr. Use \(5280\) ft \(= 1\) mile.
Part (a) — Angular Speed
\[\omega = \dfrac{800 \text{ rev}}{1 \text{ min}} \times \dfrac{2\pi \text{ rad}}{1 \text{ rev}} = 1600\pi \approx 5026.5 \text{ rad/min}\]
Part (b) — Set Up Linear Speed (Convert as You Go)
\[v = \dfrac{800 \text{ rev}}{1 \text{ min}} \times \dfrac{24\pi \text{ in}}{1 \text{ rev}} \times \dfrac{60 \text{ min}}{1 \text{ hr}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{ ft}}{12 \text{ in}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{ mi}}{5280 \text{ ft}}\]
Diameter is 24 in, so the circumference is \(24\pi\) in. Then chain conversions: min→hr, in→ft, ft→mi.
Part (b) — Cancel and Simplify
\[v = \dfrac{800 \cdot 24\pi \cdot 60}{12 \cdot 5280} = \dfrac{1{,}152{,}000\,\pi}{63{,}360}\]
Final Answer
\[v \approx 57.1 \text{ mph}\]
