2.1 Quadratic Functions
Objective: Classify polynomial functions, identify the key features of a parabola, convert between vertex form and standard form, and solve quadratic-type equations.

Reference
Polynomial Functions
A polynomial function is classified by its degree — the largest exponent on the variable.
EquationDegreeType
\(f(x) = c\) ____0 ________constant
\(f(x) = mx + b\) ____1 ________linear
\(f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c\) ____2 ________quadratic
\(f(x) = ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d\) ____3 ________cubic
\(f(x) = ax^4 + bx^3 + cx^2 + dx + e\) ____4 ________quartic
Leading Coefficient
The coefficient \(a\) on the highest-degree term. It controls end behavior and (for quadratics) whether the parabola opens up or down.
Constant Term
The term with no variable. It is always the \(y\)-intercept of the polynomial: \(f(0)\).
This Section
We focus on degree-2 polynomials — quadratic functions. Their graphs are parabolas.
Concept
Anatomy of a Parabola
The graph of every quadratic function is a parabola. It has five features worth naming.
Key Features
Vertex (red) sits on the axis of symmetry. The parabola crosses the x-axis at the x-intercepts (green) and the y-axis at the y-intercept (blue).
Opens Up vs. Down
If \(a > 0\), the parabola opens up and the vertex is a minimum. If \(a < 0\), it opens down and the vertex is a maximum.
Wide vs. Narrow
The larger \(|a|\) is, the narrower the parabola. The smaller \(|a|\) is, the wider.
Symmetry
Every parabola is a mirror image across its axis of symmetry. Two points equidistant from the axis share the same \(y\)-value.

Concept
Standard Form \(\to\) Vertex Form
Goal: rewrite \(ax^2 + bx + c\) as \(a(x-h)^2 + k\). The procedure is completing the square.
Step 1 — Factor out \(a\) from the \(x\)-terms
\[ax^2 + bx + c = a\!\left(x^2 + \tfrac{b}{a}x\right) + c\]
Step 2 — Take half the inner coefficient, square it
\[\text{half of }\tfrac{b}{a} \;=\; \tfrac{b}{2a}, \qquad \left(\tfrac{b}{2a}\right)^{\!2} = \tfrac{b^2}{4a^2}\]
Step 3 — Add and subtract it inside the parentheses
\[a\!\left(x^2 + \tfrac{b}{a}x + \tfrac{b^2}{4a^2} - \tfrac{b^2}{4a^2}\right) + c\]
Step 4 — Factor the perfect-square trinomial
\[a\!\left(x + \tfrac{b}{2a}\right)^{\!2} - \tfrac{b^2}{4a} + c\]
The first three terms inside form a perfect square. The leftover \(-\tfrac{b^2}{4a^2}\) gets multiplied by \(a\) on the way out.
Result — Vertex Form
\[f(x) = a(x-h)^2 + k \quad\text{with}\quad h = -\tfrac{b}{2a},\quad k = c - \tfrac{b^2}{4a}\]
Example 1
Convert to Vertex Form and Sketch
Given \(f(x) = 2x^2 + 8x + 7\), write the function in vertex form, identify the vertex, and sketch the graph.
Step 1 — Factor 2 from the \(x\)-terms
\[f(x) = 2(x^2 + 4x) + 7\]
Step 2 — Complete the square
\[f(x) = 2(x^2 + 4x + 4 - 4) + 7\]
Half of \(4\) is \(2\); squared is \(4\). Add and subtract \(4\) inside the parentheses.
Step 3 — Factor and distribute
\[f(x) = 2(x+2)^2 - 8 + 7\]
Vertex Form
\[f(x) = 2(x+2)^2 - 1, \qquad \text{vertex} = (-2,\, -1)\]
Sketch — opens up, narrow (\(a = 2\))
Example 2
Find the Equation from Vertex and a Point
Write the standard form of the equation of the parabola whose vertex is \((1,\, 2)\) and that passes through the point \((3,\, -6)\).
Step 1 — Start in vertex form
\[f(x) = a(x-1)^2 + 2\]
We know \(h=1\) and \(k=2\) from the vertex. The leading coefficient \(a\) is the unknown.
Step 2 — Use the point \((3, -6)\) to find \(a\)
\[-6 = a(3-1)^2 + 2\]
Step 3 — Solve for \(a\)
\[-6 = 4a + 2 \;\Longrightarrow\; 4a = -8 \;\Longrightarrow\; a = -2\]
Step 4 — Vertex form
\[f(x) = -2(x-1)^2 + 2\]
Standard Form — Expand
\[f(x) = -2(x^2 - 2x + 1) + 2 = -2x^2 + 4x\]
Example 3
Application — Sophia's Food Truck
Sophia has a food truck and sells tacos. Her daily costs are approximated by \(C(x) = x^2 - 40x + 610\), where \(C(x)\) is the cost in dollars to sell \(x\) tacos. (a) Find the number of tacos she must sell to minimize her cost. (b) What is the minimum cost?
Identify the Setup
\(C(x)\) is a parabola with \(a = 1 > 0\), so it opens up and the vertex is the minimum. The \(x\)-coordinate of the vertex tells us how many tacos minimize cost.
(a) Find the vertex \(x\)-coordinate
\[x = -\frac{b}{2a} = -\frac{-40}{2(1)} = \frac{40}{2} = 20\]
Answer (a)
\[\text{Sophia must sell } 20 \text{ tacos to minimize cost.}\]
(b) Evaluate \(C(20)\)
\[C(20) = (20)^2 - 40(20) + 610 = 400 - 800 + 610 = 210\]
Answer (b)
\[\text{Minimum cost} = \$210\]
Reference
Quadratic-Type Equations
A polynomial that isn't degree 2 can still factor like one. Look for the pattern, then substitute.
examples
\[x^{10} + 11x^5 + 28 = (x^5 + 4)(x^5 + 7)\]
\[x^{26} - 2x^{13} + 1 = (x^{13} - 1)(x^{13} - 1)\]
\[x^{100} + 3x^{50} - 10 = (x^{50} + 5)(x^{50} - 2)\]
Clue 1 — Three Terms
The equation has exactly three terms (a trinomial).
Clue 2 — Exponent Pattern
The exponent on the first term is twice the exponent on the second term.
Clue 3 — Constant Tail
The third term is a constant (no variable).
Strategy
Substitute \(u\) for the middle-term variable expression (e.g., \(u = x^5\)). The equation becomes \(au^2 + bu + c = 0\) — a familiar quadratic. Solve for \(u\), then back-substitute to solve for \(x\).
Example 4
Solve a Quadratic-Type Equation
Solve \(x^6 + 7x^3 = 8\).
Step 1 — Set equal to zero
\[x^6 + 7x^3 - 8 = 0\]
Step 2 — Recognize the pattern
\[\underbrace{x^6}_{(x^3)^2} + 7\underbrace{x^3}_{x^3} - 8 = 0\]
The first exponent (\(6\)) is twice the second (\(3\)). Quadratic in \(x^3\).
Step 3 — Substitute \(u = x^3\)
\[u^2 + 7u - 8 = 0\]
Step 4 — Factor and solve for \(u\)
\[(u + 8)(u - 1) = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; u = -8 \;\text{ or }\; u = 1\]
Step 5 — Back-substitute \(u = x^3\)
\[x^3 = -8 \;\Longrightarrow\; x = -2 \qquad x^3 = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; x = 1\]
Solution
\[x = -2,\; 1\]
Review
Can You Now...?
Use this checklist to confirm you've mastered the section.
Classify by Degree
Identify whether a polynomial is constant, linear, quadratic, cubic, or quartic — by counting the highest power.
Name the Features
Locate the vertex, axis of symmetry, \(x\)-intercepts, and \(y\)-intercept on any parabola.
Convert Forms
Take a quadratic in standard form and rewrite it in vertex form by completing the square.
Build From a Vertex
Start from \(a(x-h)^2 + k\), use a known point to solve for \(a\), and expand to standard form.
Apply to Max/Min
Use \(x = -\tfrac{b}{2a}\) to find the input that minimizes (or maximizes) a real-world quadratic, then evaluate to find the optimal value.
Spot Quadratic Type
Recognize the three-term pattern with doubled exponents, substitute \(u\), solve, then back-substitute.
Vertex form tells you where the parabola sits. Standard form tells you where it crosses the \(y\)-axis. Both describe the same parabola — pick the form that matches the question.
