6.5 Counting Principles
Objective: Use the counting principle, permutations, combinations, and non-distinct permutations to count outcomes efficiently.
counting principlepermutationscombinationsorder mattersnon-distinct objects
Concept
The Fundamental Counting Principle
Let \(A\) and \(B\) be two events.
  • The first event \(A\) can occur in ________\(m\) different ways.
  • After \(A\) has occurred, \(B\) can occur in ________\(n\) different ways.
  • The number of ways that the two events can occur is ________\(m \times n\).
Fill in the blanks on your notes as we click.
Sandwich Choices
Bill chooses one of \(4\) meats and one of \(3\) breads.
Multiply the Choices
\[4 \cdot 3 = 12\text{ sandwiches}\]
General Rule
\[\text{Total outcomes} = (\text{ways for step 1})(\text{ways for step 2})\]
When to Use It
Use the counting principle when a problem is built from sequential decisions.
Concept
Quick Counting-Principle Examples
Airport Codes

Three-letter airport codes allow repetition.

\[26 \cdot 26 \cdot 26 = 17576\]
Phone Numbers

The first digit cannot be \(0\) or \(1\), so it has \(8\) choices.

\[8 \cdot 10^6 = 8000000\]
Concept
Which Counting Method Fits?
Before writing a formula, decide what the problem is really asking you to count: a process, an arrangement, a selection, or an arrangement with repeated objects.
Sequential Decisions
Use the counting principle when the outcome is built step by step: choose this, then choose that.
Arrange or Rank
Use a permutation when order changes the outcome, such as race finishes, lineups, or officer positions.
Choose a Group
Use a combination when order does not matter, such as committees, toppings, or lottery picks.
Repeated Identical Objects
Use a non-distinct permutation when some objects repeat, so swapping identical objects does not create a new arrangement.
Ask Yourself First
Does order matter? Are the choices made in stages? Are some objects identical? The answers decide the method.
Reference
Permutations
meaning
\[\text{Order matters}\]
formula
\[{}_nP_r = \dfrac{n!}{(n-r)!}\]
  • A permutation is an arrangement of objects in a ________specific order where changing the sequence creates a different result.
Ribbon Example
Awarding blue, red, white, and yellow ribbons to four students gives \(4! = 24\) possible orders.
Example 1
Ranking Horses in a Race
Eight horses are running in a race. In how many ways can the horses finish 1st, 2nd, and 3rd?
Order Matters
  • 1st place is different from 2nd place.
  • 2nd place is different from 3rd place.
Use a Permutation
\[{}_8P_3 = 8 \cdot 7 \cdot 6 = 336\]
If All Eight Are Ranked
\[{}_8P_8 = 8! = 40320\]
Answer
\[\text{Top three finishes: }336\]
Reference
Combinations
meaning
\[\text{Order does not matter}\]
formula
\[\binom{n}{r} = \dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\]
  • A combination is a way of selecting items from a group where the order of selection ________does not matter.
Combination Examples
Committees, pizza toppings, and lottery numbers are all combination situations.
Permutation Examples
Lineups, shelf order, and race results are permutation situations.
Concept
Combination Examples
Pizza Special

Choose \(4\) toppings from \(12\).

\[\binom{12}{4} = 495\]
Packing T-Shirts

Choose \(5\) shirts from \(16\).

\[\binom{16}{5} = 4368\]
Example 2
Committee Counting
A committee of \(6\) people will be formed from \(7\) students and \(5\) teachers. Find the number of committees if (a) any \(6\) people may be chosen and (b) exactly four members must be students.
Part (a): Any Six People
\[\binom{12}{6} = 924\]
Part (b): Split the Choice by Group
\[\binom{7}{4}\binom{5}{2}\]
Choose \(4\) students and \(2\) teachers.
Multiply the Independent Choices
\[\binom{7}{4}\binom{5}{2} = 35 \cdot 10 = 350\]
Your Turn
Practice: Permutations or Combinations?
A club has \(10\) members. How many ways can they choose a president, vice president, and treasurer?
Hint
These are three different offices, so choosing the same people in a different order gives a different result.
Answer
\[\text{Order matters} \Rightarrow {}_{10}P_3 = 10 \cdot 9 \cdot 8 = 720\]
Concept
When Repeats Change the Count
A regular permutation counts every position swap as different. When some objects are identical, that overcounts the real number of distinguishable arrangements.
Distinct Objects
For \(A,B,C\), all \(3! = 6\) orders are different because every letter is unique.
Repeated Objects
For \(A,A,B\), the swaps of the two \(A\)'s do not make a new arrangement, so \(3!\) is too large.
Fix the Overcount
Divide by the factorial of each repeated group: \[\dfrac{n!}{n_1!n_2!\cdots n_k!}\]
Why Division Works
If a letter appears \(3\) times, those \(3!\) swaps all look the same. Divide by \(3!\) so each distinct arrangement is counted once.
Example 3
Non-Distinct Permutations
How many distinguishable arrangements of the letters in TATTOOS are possible?
Count the Repeated Letters
  • Total letters: \(7\)
  • \(T\) appears \(3\) times.
  • \(O\) appears \(2\) times.
Start with \(7!\) and Divide Out the Repeats
\[\dfrac{7!}{3!\,2!}\]
Simplify
\[\dfrac{5040}{12} = 420\]
Review
Can You Now...?
Use this checklist to decide which counting tool belongs in a problem before you start computing.
Recognize a Process
Use the counting principle when the outcome is built in stages and each stage has a fixed number of choices.
Recognize Order
Decide whether changing the order creates a new outcome. If it does, think permutation.
Recognize a Selection
Use combinations when the task is choosing a group and the order of the chosen objects does not matter.
Recognize Repeats
Use non-distinct permutations when repeated identical objects make ordinary \(n!\) count too many arrangements.
Explain the Method
Say why you chose counting principle, permutation, combination, or non-distinct permutation before writing the formula.
Check Reasonableness
Permutation answers should usually be larger than combination answers with the same \(n\) and \(r\) because order creates more outcomes.
If you are unsure which formula to use, pause and classify the problem before calculating.
