4.4 Trig Functions: The Unit Circle
Objective: Use the unit circle to evaluate all six trigonometric functions for standard angles, and apply periodicity and even/odd symmetry.
unit circle coords six trig values ASTC signs periodicity even / odd functions
Concept
Before We Build the Unit Circle — Special Right Triangles
The 45-45-90 and 30-60-90 triangles supply the side ratios for every special angle on the unit circle.
45-45-90 — Isosceles Right
Rule
\[\text{hypotenuse} = \sqrt{2}\,\cdot\,\text{leg}\]
30-60-90 — Half-Equilateral
Rules
\[\text{hypotenuse} = 2\,\cdot\,\text{short leg}\]\[\text{long leg} = \sqrt{3}\,\cdot\,\text{short leg}\]
Concept
Q1 Coordinates at \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{6}\) (30°)
Drop a perpendicular from the point on the unit circle to the \(x\)-axis. The triangle inside is a 30-60-90 with hypotenuse 1.
From the 30-60-90 with hyp = 1
short leg (across from 30°) = \(\tfrac{1}{2}\)
long leg (across from 60°) = \(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\)
Coordinate Pair
\[\left(\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\;\dfrac{1}{2}\right)\]
Pattern
The horizontal leg is the \(x\)-coordinate, the vertical leg is the \(y\)-coordinate. We'll see why on slide 6.
Concept
Q1 Coordinates at \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{4}\) (45°)
At 45°, the triangle is 45-45-90 with hypotenuse 1, so both legs are equal — and equal to \(\sqrt{2}/2\).
From the 45-45-90 with hyp = 1
Each leg = \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\)
(rationalize the denominator)
Coordinate Pair
\[\left(\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\;\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\]
Symmetry
\(\pi/4\) is the unique angle in Q1 where \(x = y\). That's why the sine and cosine match here.
Concept
Q1 Coordinates at \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{3}\) (60°)
Same 30-60-90 triangle as \(\pi/6\), but now we're looking at the \(60^\circ\) angle at the origin. The legs swap roles.
From the 30-60-90 with hyp = 1
short leg (now horizontal) = \(\tfrac{1}{2}\)
long leg (now vertical) = \(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\)
Coordinate Pair
\[\left(\dfrac{1}{2},\;\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)\]
Cofunction Echo
Notice \(\pi/3\) and \(\pi/6\) are complementary, and their coordinates swap: \((\sqrt{3}/2,\,1/2) \;\leftrightarrow\; (1/2,\,\sqrt{3}/2)\).
Reference
The Unit Circle — Memorize This
Each labeled point on the unit circle shows the radian measure and its coordinates \((\cos\theta,\,\sin\theta)\). Reflections across the axes propagate the Q1 values to the other three quadrants — and signs follow the quadrant they land in. For degree equivalents see the 4.2 reference.
Concept
What Do These Coordinates Actually Mean?
On the unit circle, the point at angle \(\theta\) is exactly \((\cos\theta,\;\sin\theta)\). Cosine reads the \(x\)-coordinate; sine reads the \(y\)-coordinate. The other four functions are built from those two.
Primary
\[\cos\theta = x \qquad \sin\theta = y\]
Tangent
\[\tan\theta = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = \dfrac{y}{x} \quad (x \ne 0)\]
Reciprocals
\[\csc\theta = \dfrac{1}{y} \qquad \sec\theta = \dfrac{1}{x} \qquad \cot\theta = \dfrac{x}{y}\]
Every Value, One Lookup
If you can read the \((x, y)\) at any angle on the unit circle, you can evaluate all six trig functions there with no calculator.
Watch the Domain
\(\tan\theta\) and \(\sec\theta\) are undefined when \(x = 0\) (at \(\theta = \pi/2,\, 3\pi/2,\dots\)). \(\csc\theta\) and \(\cot\theta\) are undefined when \(y = 0\) (at \(\theta = 0,\, \pi,\, 2\pi,\dots\)).
Reference
Signs by Quadrant — "All Students Take Calculus"
In each quadrant, exactly one of \(\sin\), \(\cos\), \(\tan\) is positive — along with its reciprocal. The mnemonic ASTC tells you which.
ASTC Wheel
A — All
In Q1 every trig function is positive (both \(x\) and \(y\) are positive).
S — Sine
In Q2 only sine (and its reciprocal csc) are positive. Cosine and tangent are negative.
T — Tangent
In Q3 only tangent (and cot) are positive. Sine and cosine are both negative — and their ratio is positive.
C — Cosine
In Q4 only cosine (and sec) are positive. Sine and tangent are negative.
Example 1
Six Trig Functions of \(\dfrac{5\pi}{4}\)
Write down the six trigonometric functions of \(\theta = \dfrac{5\pi}{4}\).
Step 1 — Locate the Angle and Read Its Coordinates
\[\dfrac{5\pi}{4} \text{ is in Quadrant III; reference angle } \dfrac{\pi}{4}\]\[(\cos\theta,\;\sin\theta) = \left(-\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\;-\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\]
In Q3 both \(x\) and \(y\) are negative (S→sine only in Q2).
Step 2 — Primary Trig Functions
\[\sin\dfrac{5\pi}{4} = -\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \qquad \cos\dfrac{5\pi}{4} = -\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \qquad \tan\dfrac{5\pi}{4} = \dfrac{-\sqrt{2}/2}{-\sqrt{2}/2} = 1\]
Step 3 — Reciprocals
\[\csc\dfrac{5\pi}{4} = -\sqrt{2} \qquad \sec\dfrac{5\pi}{4} = -\sqrt{2} \qquad \cot\dfrac{5\pi}{4} = 1\]
All Six Values
\(\sin = -\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\)  |  \(\cos = -\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\)  |  \(\tan = 1\)
\(\csc = -\sqrt{2}\)  |  \(\sec = -\sqrt{2}\)  |  \(\cot = 1\)
Example 2
Six Trig Functions at \(\theta = -\dfrac{\pi}{3}\)
Write down the six trig functions at \(\theta = -\dfrac{\pi}{3}\).
Step 1 — Find a Coterminal Angle
\[-\dfrac{\pi}{3} + 2\pi = \dfrac{5\pi}{3}\]
The terminal side lies in Quadrant IV. By ASTC, cosine is positive there; sine and tangent are negative.
Step 2 — Read the Coterminal Coordinates
\[\left(\cos,\;\sin\right) = \left(\dfrac{1}{2},\;-\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)\]
Step 3 — Build All Six
\[\sin = -\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \qquad \cos = \dfrac{1}{2} \qquad \tan = \dfrac{-\sqrt{3}/2}{1/2} = -\sqrt{3}\]
\[\csc = -\dfrac{2\sqrt{3}}{3} \qquad \sec = 2 \qquad \cot = -\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\]
All Six Values
\(\sin = -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\)  |  \(\cos = \tfrac{1}{2}\)  |  \(\tan = -\sqrt{3}\)
\(\csc = -\tfrac{2\sqrt{3}}{3}\)  |  \(\sec = 2\)  |  \(\cot = -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\)
Concept
Periodicity — Going Around Doesn't Change the Value
Add or subtract a full revolution (\(2\pi\)) and you land back on the same point of the unit circle. Sine and cosine repeat with period \(2\pi\); tangent and cotangent repeat with period \(\pi\).
Sine / Cosine
\[\sin(\theta + 2\pi k) = \sin\theta \qquad \cos(\theta + 2\pi k) = \cos\theta \quad (k \in \mathbb{Z})\]
Tangent / Cotangent
\[\tan(\theta + \pi k) = \tan\theta \qquad \cot(\theta + \pi k) = \cot\theta\]
Strategy for Big Angles
If the angle is bigger than \(2\pi\) (or less than \(-2\pi\)), subtract or add \(2\pi\) until you're back in \([0, 2\pi)\), then use the unit circle.
Why Tangent Period Is Smaller
Going halfway around the circle flips the sign of both \(x\) and \(y\), so their ratio \(y/x\) is unchanged. Tangent only "knows" half-revolutions.
Example 3
Evaluate Beyond One Revolution
Evaluate \(\sin\!\left(\dfrac{13\pi}{6}\right)\) and \(\cos\!\left(\dfrac{13\pi}{6}\right)\).
Step 1 — Reduce by One Full Turn
\[\dfrac{13\pi}{6} - 2\pi = \dfrac{13\pi}{6} - \dfrac{12\pi}{6} = \dfrac{\pi}{6}\]
So \(\dfrac{13\pi}{6}\) is coterminal with \(\dfrac{\pi}{6}\).
Step 2 — Read the Coterminal Coordinates
\[(\cos,\;\sin) = \left(\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\;\dfrac{1}{2}\right)\]
Final Answer
\[\sin\!\left(\dfrac{13\pi}{6}\right) = \dfrac{1}{2} \qquad \cos\!\left(\dfrac{13\pi}{6}\right) = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\]
Example 4
Calculator — When Exact Values Don't Apply
Evaluate with a calculator (round to three decimal places): (a) \(\sin\!\left(\dfrac{2\pi}{3}\right)\) and (b) \(\cot(1.5)\).
Part (a) — Set Calculator to Radian Mode
\[\sin\!\left(\dfrac{2\pi}{3}\right) \approx 0.866\]
This is the exact value \(\sqrt{3}/2\), now shown as a decimal. Q2 → sine positive.
Part (b) — Cotangent Isn't a Calculator Button
\[\cot(1.5) = \dfrac{1}{\tan(1.5)} = \dfrac{\cos(1.5)}{\sin(1.5)} \approx 0.071\]
No "cot" key — rewrite as a reciprocal or quotient, then use \(\tan\), or \(\sin\) and \(\cos\).
Final Answers
\[\sin\!\left(\dfrac{2\pi}{3}\right) \approx 0.866 \qquad \cot(1.5) \approx 0.071\]
Concept
Even and Odd Trig Functions
Replacing \(\theta\) with \(-\theta\) reflects the point on the unit circle across the \(x\)-axis. The \(x\)-coordinate is unchanged; the \(y\)-coordinate flips sign. That makes cosine (and its reciprocal sec) even, while the other four are odd.
Even
\[\cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta \qquad \sec(-\theta) = \sec\theta\]
Graph is symmetric about the \(y\)-axis: \(f(-x) = f(x)\).
Odd
\[\sin(-\theta) = -\sin\theta \qquad \csc(-\theta) = -\csc\theta\]
\[\tan(-\theta) = -\tan\theta \qquad \cot(-\theta) = -\cot\theta\]
Graph is symmetric about the origin: \(f(-x) = -f(x)\).
Geometric Memory
"Cosine is even because cosine reads the \(x\)-coordinate, and flipping the angle's sign doesn't change \(x\). Sine reads \(y\), and \(y\) flips — so sine is odd."
Example 5
Verify Using Even / Odd and Pythagorean
Use trig identities to transform the left side into the right side: \[\sin(-x)\,\tan(-x) + \cos(-x) = \sec(x).\]
Step 1 — Apply Even / Odd
\[(-\sin x)\,(-\tan x) + \cos x = \sin x\,\tan x + \cos x\]
Sine and tangent are odd (sign flips); cosine is even (sign stays).
Step 2 — Rewrite tan as sin/cos
\[\sin x \cdot \dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x} + \cos x = \dfrac{\sin^{2} x}{\cos x} + \cos x\]
Step 3 — Common Denominator
\[\dfrac{\sin^{2} x}{\cos x} + \dfrac{\cos^{2} x}{\cos x} = \dfrac{\sin^{2} x + \cos^{2} x}{\cos x}\]
Step 4 — Pythagorean Identity
\[\dfrac{\sin^{2} x + \cos^{2} x}{\cos x} = \dfrac{1}{\cos x} = \sec x\]
Verified
\[\sin(-x)\,\tan(-x) + \cos(-x) = \sec(x) \;\;\checkmark\]
