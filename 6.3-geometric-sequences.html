Test for Geometric
A sequence is geometric if and only if \(\dfrac{a_n}{a_{n-1}}\) is the same for every pair of consecutive terms.
Ratio Can Be Negative
When \(r < 0\), the terms alternate in sign.
Growth vs. Decay
If \(|r| > 1\), the terms grow in size. If \(0 < |r| < 1\), the terms shrink toward \(0\).
Reference
Geometric Sequence Formulas
ratio
\[r = \dfrac{a_n}{a_{n-1}}\]
nth term
\[a_n = a_1r^{\,n-1}\]
finite sum
\[S_n = \dfrac{a_1(1-r^n)}{1-r}, \quad r \ne 1\]
infinite sum
\[S_{\infty} = \dfrac{a_1}{1-r}, \quad |r| < 1\]
The geometric mean between two positive numbers \(a\) and \(b\) is \(\sqrt{ab}\).
Quick Check
Recognizing a Geometric Rule
Write an explicit formula for the sequence \(3, 6, 12, 24, \dots\).
Find the Common Ratio
\[\dfrac{6}{3} = \dfrac{12}{6} = \dfrac{24}{12} = 2\]
Use the nth-Term Formula
\[a_n = a_1r^{n-1} = 3(2)^{n-1}\]
Answer
\[a_n = 3\cdot 2^{n-1}\]

Example 2
Writing a Geometric Explicit Formula
Write an equation for the \(n\)th term of the geometric sequence where \(a_6 = 4\) and \(r = 3\).
Use the nth-Term Formula
\[a_6 = a_1 \cdot r^5 \implies 4 = a_1 \cdot 3^5 = 243\,a_1\]
Solve for \(a_1\)
\[a_1 = \frac{4}{243}\]
Answer
\[a_n = \frac{4}{243}\cdot 3^{\,n-1}\]

Concept
Finite and Infinite Geometric Sums
Multiplying a geometric sum by the common ratio makes almost every term line up, which is why the finite-sum formula works so cleanly.
  • If \(|r| \ge 1\), the series __________ diverges (no finite sum).
Partial Sums Approaching a Limit (6 + 3 + 1.5 + 0.75 + …)
Finite Sum (derivation)
Multiply by \(r\) and subtract to collapse middle terms: \[(1-r)\,S_n = a_1 - a_1 r^n\] \[S_n = \dfrac{a_1\!\left(1 - r^n\right)}{1-r}\]

Convergence (\(|r| < 1\))
The partial sums \(S_n\) settle toward one single number — their limit: \[S = \dfrac{a_1}{1-r}\] \(S\) is the value \(S_n\) gets as close to as we want by taking enough terms.

Divergence (\(|r| \ge 1\))
The terms do not shrink to zero, so the partial sums grow without bound (or oscillate). The series diverges — no finite sum exists.

Example 4
Summing a Finite Geometric Series
Find the exact sum of the first 12 terms of \(8, 4, 2, \dots\).
Identify the Data
  • \(a_1 = 8\)
  • \(r = \dfrac{1}{2}\)
  • \(n = 12\)
Use the Finite-Sum Formula
\[S_{12} = \dfrac{8\left(1-\left(\frac12\right)^{12}\right)}{1-\frac12}\]
Simplify Exactly
\[S_{12} = 16\left(1 - \dfrac{1}{4096}\right) = \dfrac{4095}{256}\]
Example 5
Solving for the Number of Terms
A geometric series has first term \(1\), common ratio \(3\), and finite sum \(29{,}524\). Find \(n\).
Write the Sum Formula
\[29{,}524 = \dfrac{1(1-3^n)}{1-3} = \dfrac{3^n - 1}{2}\]
Solve for \(3^n\)
\[59{,}048 = 3^n - 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 59{,}049 = 3^n\]
Recognize the Power
\[59{,}049 = 3^{10}\]
Answer
\[n = 10\]
Example 6
Evaluating a Sum in Sigma Notation
Find the sum: \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{12} 4(0.3)^n\)
Identify the Data
  • \(a_1 = 4(0.3)^1 = 1.2\)
  • \(r = 0.3\)
  • \(n = 12\)
Tip
To find \(a_1\) from sigma notation, plug the starting index into the formula: \(a_1 = 4(0.3)^1 = 1.2\).
Use the Finite-Sum Formula
\[S_{12} = \dfrac{1.2\left(1-0.3^{12}\right)}{1-0.3}\]
Answer
\[S_{12} \approx 1.714\]
Example 7
Infinite Geometric Series
Find the sum of the infinite geometric series \(6 + 3 + 1.5 + 0.75 + \dots\).
Check the Ratio
\[r = \dfrac{3}{6} = \dfrac12\]
\(|r| < 1\), so the infinite sum exists.
Use the Infinite-Sum Formula
\[S_{\infty} = \dfrac{a_1}{1-r} = \dfrac{6}{1-\frac12}\]
Answer
\[S_{\infty} = 12\]
Example 8
Alternating Infinite Series
Find the sum of \(\displaystyle 3 - \frac{3}{2} + \frac{3}{4} - \frac{3}{8} + \cdots\), if it exists.
Identify \(a_1\) and \(r\)
\[a_1 = 3, \qquad r = \frac{-3/2}{3} = -\frac{1}{2}\]
\(|r| = \frac{1}{2} < 1\), so the series converges.
Apply the Infinite-Sum Formula
\[S = \frac{3}{1-\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)} = \frac{3}{\frac{3}{2}}\]
Answer
\[S = 2\]
Example 9
Infinite Series in Sigma Notation
Find the sum: \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 3\!\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{\!n}\)
Identify \(a_1\) and \(r\)
  • \(a_1 = 3 \cdot \frac{2}{3} = 2\)
  • \(r = \frac{2}{3}\), and \(|r| < 1\) so the series converges.
Apply the Infinite-Sum Formula
\[S = \frac{2}{1-\frac{2}{3}} = \frac{2}{\frac{1}{3}}\]
Answer
\[S = 6\]
Think About It
Every repeating decimal is a geometric series! For example, \(0.666\ldots = \frac{6}{10} + \frac{6}{100} + \frac{6}{1000} + \cdots\) with \(r = \frac{1}{10}\), giving \(S = \frac{6/10}{1-1/10} = \frac{2}{3}\).

Where does \(S_n = \dfrac{a_1(1-r^n)}{1-r}\) come from?
A step-by-step derivation — the trick is to multiply by \(r\) and subtract.
  1. Step 1 · Write the sum
    \[S_n = a_1 + a_1 r + a_1 r^2 + a_1 r^3 + \dots + a_1 r^{n-1}\]
    Every term is the previous one multiplied by \(r\). Count carefully: there are \(n\) terms, and the last one is \(a_1 r^{n-1}\) (not \(a_1 r^n\)).
  2. Step 2 · Multiply every term by \(r\)
    \[r S_n = a_1 r + a_1 r^2 + a_1 r^3 + \dots + a_1 r^{n-1} + a_1 r^{n}\]
    Each power of \(r\) shifts up by one. Notice how most of \(rS_n\) looks just like most of \(S_n\) — that's what makes the next step collapse.
  3. Step 3 · Subtract \(r S_n\) from \(S_n\) — middle terms cancel
    \[S_n - r S_n = a_1 + \underbrace{(a_1 r - a_1 r)}_{0} + \underbrace{(a_1 r^2 - a_1 r^2)}_{0} + \dots + \underbrace{(a_1 r^{n-1} - a_1 r^{n-1})}_{0} - a_1 r^n\]
    Every matching power of \(r\) between the two sums cancels. Only the very first term of \(S_n\) (which is \(a_1\)) and the very last term of \(rS_n\) (which is \(a_1 r^n\)) survive.
  4. Step 4 · Factor both sides
    \[(1 - r)\, S_n = a_1 \left(1 - r^n\right)\]
    Pull \(S_n\) out on the left (it's in both terms on the left side). Pull \(a_1\) out on the right.
  5. Step 5 · Solve for \(S_n\)
    \[S_n = \dfrac{a_1 \left(1 - r^n\right)}{1 - r} \quad (r \ne 1)\]
    Divide both sides by \(1 - r\). Important: this is why the formula fails when \(r = 1\) — you'd be dividing by zero. If \(r = 1\), every term equals \(a_1\), so \(S_n = n \cdot a_1\) directly.
What does "\(S\) is the limit of the partial sums" mean?
Intuitive version — we'll formalize "limit" in calculus, but you already have the idea.
  1. The setup
    \[S_n = a_1 + a_1 r + a_1 r^2 + \dots + a_1 r^{n-1}\]
    \(S_n\) is the sum of the first \(n\) terms — a partial sum. As \(n\) grows, we add more and more terms.
  2. Watch the partial sums for \(6 + 3 + 1.5 + 0.75 + \dots\) (\(a_1 = 6,\; r = \tfrac12\))
    \(n\)1235102050
    \(S_n\)6910.511.62511.98811.99999\(\approx 12\)
    distance \(|12 - S_n|\)631.50.3750.0120.00001\(\approx 0\)
    Each new term cuts the remaining distance to 12 in half. The partial sums never actually reach 12 — but they get as close as you want.
  3. The intuitive meaning of "limit"
    Saying "\(S_n\) has limit \(S\)" means: pick any target distance you like — maybe 0.1, or 0.0001, or \(10^{-50}\) — and you can always find a big enough \(n\) so that \(S_n\) is within that distance of \(S\), and stays there for every larger \(n\).

    Think of zooming in: no matter how tight you make your target zone around \(S\), eventually all the \(S_n\) fall inside it.
  4. Why the formula \(S = \dfrac{a_1}{1-r}\) works when \(|r|<1\)
    \[S_n = \dfrac{a_1(1 - r^n)}{1 - r} \;\; \xrightarrow{\;n \to \infty\;} \;\; \dfrac{a_1(1 - 0)}{1 - r} = \dfrac{a_1}{1 - r}\]
    Because \(|r| < 1\), multiplying \(r\) by itself over and over makes \(r^n\) shrink toward 0. The only piece of the formula that was still depending on \(n\) vanishes, leaving a single finite number.
  5. For our example
    \[S = \dfrac{6}{1 - \tfrac12} = \dfrac{6}{\tfrac12} = 12\]
    So \(6 + 3 + 1.5 + 0.75 + \dots = 12\) — not as an arithmetic fact about any finite sum, but as the limit of the partial sums.
Why \(|r| \ge 1\) breaks the infinite sum
The core reason: the terms themselves never shrink to zero, so the partial sums can't settle.
  1. The test — always check \(|r|\) first
    \[\text{Infinite sum exists} \;\iff\; |r| < 1\]
    If \(|r| \ge 1\), the terms \(a_1 r^n\) either stay the same size or grow, so the partial sums can never stop moving by less than \(|a_1|\) at a time.
  2. Case 1 · \(|r| > 1\) — terms grow, sum blows up
    \[1 + 2 + 4 + 8 + 16 + \dots \quad (a_1 = 1,\; r = 2)\]
    After 10 terms: \(S_{10} = 1023\). After 20 terms: \(S_{20} = 1{,}048{,}575\). Each new term is bigger than all the previous ones combined, so the partial sums rocket to infinity. No finite sum.
  3. Case 2 · \(r = 1\) — every term is the same
    \[a_1 + a_1 + a_1 + a_1 + \dots \;=\; n \cdot a_1\]
    Every term equals \(a_1\), so the partial sum is just \(n a_1\). If \(a_1 \ne 0\), this grows without bound. Also note: the formula \(\dfrac{a_1(1 - r^n)}{1 - r}\) divides by \(1 - r = 0\) here — that's why it fails.
  4. Case 3 · \(r \le -1\) — terms flip sign but don't shrink
    \[1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + \dots \quad (a_1 = 1,\; r = -1)\]
    The partial sums oscillate: \(S_1 = 1,\; S_2 = 0,\; S_3 = 1,\; S_4 = 0, \dots\) They bounce between 1 and 0 forever without settling on any single value. If \(r < -1\) it's worse — the amplitude grows.
  5. The big idea
    A convergent series needs its terms to shrink toward zero — that's the only way the partial sums can settle on a single value. For a geometric sequence, "terms shrink" happens exactly when \(|r| < 1\). Anything else, and we're stuck.
