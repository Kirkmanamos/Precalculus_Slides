2.2 Graphs of Polynomial Functions
Objective: Identify the features that make a graph polynomial, predict end behavior from the leading term, use multiplicity to know whether the graph crosses or is tangent to the \(x\)-axis, and sketch polynomials from factored form.
continuous smooth zeros turning points end behavior multiplicity
Concept
What Makes a Graph Polynomial
  • The graph of every polynomial function is ________________continuous — no breaks, no holes, no jumps.
  • The graph has only ________________smooth, rounded turns — no sharp corners or cusps.
continuous
not continuous → not a polynomial function
smooth, rounded turns
sharp turn → not a polynomial function
Continuous means…
You can trace the entire graph without lifting your pencil. No gaps, no jumps, no holes.
Smooth means…
Every turning point is rounded — there are no sharp corners (no cusps, no kinks).
The Test
________Both properties must hold for the graph to be polynomial. Either one alone is ________not enough.
Concept
Monomial Building Blocks: \(f(x) = x^n\)
The simplest polynomials are the monomials \(f(x) = x^n\). Comparing them shows how the exponent \(n\) shapes the graph near the origin.
\(f(x) = x\)
\(f(x) = x^2\)
\(f(x) = x^3\)
\(f(x) = x^4\)
Even \(n\) (2, 4, 6, …)
U-shaped. Both ends point up. Symmetric across the \(y\)-axis. The vertex is at the origin.
Odd \(n\) (1, 3, 5, …)
S-shaped. Left end down, right end up. Passes through the origin from quadrant III to quadrant I.
Near the Origin
The greater the value of \(n\), the ________flatter the graph near the origin — and the ________steeper it climbs once \(|x| > 1\).
Warm-Up
Sketch \(f(x) = (x+1)(x-3)\)
Find the \(x\)-intercepts and sketch the graph.
Step 1 — Read the zeros off the factors
\[x + 1 = 0 \;\text{ or }\; x - 3 = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; x = -1,\; x = 3\]
Step 2 — Identify the shape
Expanding gives \(f(x) = x^2 - 2x - 3\). Degree \(2\), leading coefficient \(+1\) ⇒ parabola opening up with two \(x\)-intercepts.
Step 3 — Find the vertex (halfway between zeros)
\[x = \tfrac{-1 + 3}{2} = 1, \quad f(1) = (2)(-2) = -4 \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \text{vertex } (1,\, -4)\]
Step 4 — Sketch
Warm-Up
Sketch \(f(x) = (x+1)(x-3)(x+6)\)
Find the \(x\)-intercepts and sketch the graph.
Step 1 — Zeros from the factors
\[x = -1,\; x = 3,\; x = -6\]
Step 2 — Identify the shape
Leading term \(x \cdot x \cdot x = x^3\). Degree \(3\), positive leading coefficient ⇒ a cubic with end behavior left ↓, right ↑.
Step 3 — Sign check between zeros
Pick a test point in each interval and check the sign of \(f\): negative on \((-\infty, -6)\), positive on \((-6, -1)\), negative on \((-1, 3)\), positive on \((3, \infty)\).
Step 4 — Sketch

Example 1
Which Graph Has Leading Term \(-3x^4\)?
MULTIPLE CHOICE. Which of the following could be the graph of a polynomial whose leading term is \(-3x^4\)?
Step 1 — Read the leading term
Leading term \(-3x^4\): degree is \(4\) (even), leading coefficient is \(-3\) (negative).
Step 2 — Apply the Leading Coefficient Test
Even degree with a negative leading coefficient ⇒ both ends point down (Case 2 from the table).
Step 3 — Eliminate
Discard any option whose left and right arms don't both go down. Up-and-down or down-and-up shapes belong to odd-degree polynomials.
Answer
Choose the graph whose left arm and right arm both point downward — that is the unique shape consistent with an even degree and a negative leading coefficient.

Concept
Repeated Zeros & Multiplicity
Interactive Explorer ↗
For a polynomial, a factor of \((x - a)^k\) with \(k > 1\) means \(x = a\) is a zero of multiplicity \(k\). The multiplicity controls how the graph meets the \(x\)-axis at that zero.
\(k\) is odd → crosses
The graph passes through the \(x\)-axis.
\(k\) is even → tangent
The graph touches the \(x\)-axis and bounces back without crossing.
Higher \(k\) → flatter
The more repeats, the more slowly the curve moves through (or past) the \(x\)-axis.
rule
If \(k\) is ________odd, the graph ________crosses the \(x\)-axis at \(x = a\).
rule
If \(k\) is ________even, the graph is ________tangent to the \(x\)-axis at \(x = a\).
Example 3
\(f(x) = -(x-1)(x+2)(x-3)\)
(a) Identify the degree. (b) Identify the \(x\)-intercepts. (c) State whether the graph crosses or is tangent at each. (d) Sketch the graph.
(a) Degree
Three linear factors ⇒ degree \(\mathbf{3}\) (cubic). Leading term is \(-x^3\), so leading coefficient is \(-1\).
(b) \(x\)-intercepts
\[x = 1,\quad x = -2,\quad x = 3\]
(c) Cross or Tangent
All three factors have multiplicity \(1\) (odd) ⇒ the graph crosses the \(x\)-axis at every intercept.
End behavior check
Odd degree, negative leading ⇒ left ↑, right ↓. The graph starts high on the left, weaves through three crossings, and ends low on the right.
(d) Sketch
Example 4
\(f(x) = (x+2)^2 (x-3)^3\)
(a) Identify the degree. (b) Identify the \(x\)-intercepts. (c) State whether the graph crosses or is tangent at each. (d) Sketch the graph.
(a) Degree
Add the multiplicities: \(2 + 3 = \mathbf{5}\) (quintic). Leading term is \(x^5\), so leading coefficient is \(+1\).
(b) \(x\)-intercepts
\[x = -2 \;\;(\text{multiplicity } 2), \qquad x = 3 \;\;(\text{multiplicity } 3)\]
(c) Cross or Tangent
At \(x = -2\), multiplicity is even ⇒ tangent (touches and bounces). At \(x = 3\), multiplicity is odd ⇒ crosses, with a flatter S-curve through the axis because \(k = 3\).
End behavior check
Odd degree, positive leading ⇒ left ↓, right ↑. The graph starts low, comes up to touch \(x = -2\), drops back, dives down, then crosses through \(x = 3\) on its way up.
(d) Sketch (qualitative — vertical scale compressed)

Write an Equation from a Graph
Write an equation for the graph shown. Assume all \(x\)-intercepts are integers and the leading coefficient is \(1\) or \(-1\).
The graph
Step 1 — Read the zeros
\[x = -3,\;-1,\;1,\;2\]
Step 2 — Write the factors
\[f(x) = a\,(x+3)(x+1)(x-1)(x-2)\]

Review
Can You Now...?
Use this checklist to make sure you can sketch any polynomial in factored form.
Recognize a Polynomial Graph
Continuous everywhere with only smooth, rounded turns — no jumps, no sharp corners.
Count Zeros & Turns
A degree-\(n\) polynomial has at most \(n\) zeros and at most \(n-1\) turning points.
Predict End Behavior
Use degree (even/odd) and the sign of the leading coefficient to determine the four cases.
Use Multiplicity
Odd-power factor ⇒ crosses. Even-power factor ⇒ tangent (bounces). Higher \(k\) ⇒ flatter pass.
Sketch from Factors
Plot the zeros, decide cross-or-tangent at each, anchor the \(y\)-intercept, and draw the arms using end behavior.
Reverse Engineer
From a given graph, read the zeros (with multiplicities) and the end behavior to write \(f(x)\) in factored form.
A factored polynomial is a graph in disguise: each factor is a zero, each exponent is a multiplicity, and the leading sign is the end behavior.
