2.5 Complex Numbers
Objective: Place complex numbers in the number-set hierarchy, write any complex number in standard form \(a + bi\), add, subtract, multiply, and divide complex numbers, and solve quadratic equations that produce complex solutions.
imaginary unit \(i\) standard form conjugates complex division complex roots
Reference
The Number-Set Hierarchy
Every number you have ever met lives inside the complex numbers, \(\mathbb{C}\). The smaller sets nest inside one another from the inside out.
Nested Number Sets
Numbers like \(3\), \(-\tfrac{1}{2}\), and \(\sqrt{2}\) live somewhere inside the Real bubble. Numbers like \(5i\) and \(2 + 3i\) live in Imaginary or in the Complex layer.
Reference
Complex Numbers in Standard Form
imaginary unit
\[i = \sqrt{-1}, \qquad i^2 = -1\]
standard form
\[a + bi\quad\text{where}\quad a, b \in \mathbb{R}\]
  • The number \(a\) is called the ________real part.
  • The number \(b\) is called the ________imaginary part.
  • When \(b = 0\), the number reduces to a real number. When \(a = 0\) and \(b \neq 0\), it is called ________pure imaginary.
Powers of \(i\)
\(i^1 = i\), \(i^2 = -1\), \(i^3 = -i\), \(i^4 = 1\), then the pattern repeats every four. Use \(i^2 = -1\) to simplify products.
Treat \(i\) Like a Variable
Add, subtract, and multiply complex numbers like binomials in \(i\). Replace every \(i^2\) you see with \(-1\) at the end.
Standard Form Goal
Every answer should end as \(a + bi\) with the real and imaginary parts clearly separated — never \(i\) in a denominator.
Example 1
\((3 + 2i) + (4 - i) - (7 + i)\)
Perform the operations and write the result in standard form.
Step 1 — Distribute the subtraction
\[3 + 2i + 4 - i - 7 - i\]
Step 2 — Combine real parts
\[\underbrace{(3 + 4 - 7)}_{= 0} + \,(2i - i - i)\]
Step 3 — Combine imaginary parts
\[0 + \underbrace{(2 - 1 - 1)}_{= 0}\,i\]
Standard form
\[= 0\]
Example 2
\(3 - (-2 + 3i) + (-8 + i)\)
Watch the signs carefully when distributing the subtraction.
Step 1 — Distribute the negative
\[3 + 2 - 3i + (-8) + i\]
Step 2 — Group real and imaginary
\[(3 + 2 - 8) + (-3i + i)\]
Step 3 — Simplify each part
\[-3 + (-2i)\]
Standard form
\[-3 - 2i\]
Example 3
\(4i \,(-1 + 5i)\)
Multiply and write the result in standard form.
Step 1 — Distribute
\[4i \cdot (-1) + 4i \cdot 5i = -4i + 20i^2\]
Step 2 — Replace \(i^2 = -1\)
\[-4i + 20(-1) = -4i - 20\]
Step 3 — Put real part first
\[-20 - 4i\]
Standard form
\[-20 - 4i\]
Example 4
\((3 + 4i)^2\)
Square the binomial and write the result in standard form.
Step 1 — FOIL
\[(3 + 4i)(3 + 4i) = 9 + 12i + 12i + 16i^2\]
Step 2 — Combine middle terms
\[= 9 + 24i + 16i^2\]
Step 3 — Replace \(i^2 = -1\)
\[= 9 + 24i - 16\]
Standard form
\[-7 + 24i\]
Example 5
\(\bigl(5 - \sqrt{-9}\,\bigr)\bigl(-1 + \sqrt{-4}\,\bigr)\)
Multiply and express in the form \(a + bi\). Convert the negative radicals to \(i\)-form before distributing.
Step 1 — Simplify the radicals
\[\sqrt{-9} = 3i, \qquad \sqrt{-4} = 2i\]
Always extract the \(i\) first. Multiplying radicals after the fact would give \(\sqrt{(-9)(-4)} = \sqrt{36} = 6\) — the wrong answer.
Step 2 — Rewrite
\[(5 - 3i)(-1 + 2i)\]
Step 3 — FOIL
\[-5 + 10i + 3i - 6i^2\]
Step 4 — Replace \(i^2 = -1\) and combine
\[-5 + 13i + 6\]
Standard form
\[1 + 13i\]
Concept
Complex Conjugates
A pair of complex numbers \(a + bi\) and \(a - bi\) are called complex conjugates. Their product is always a real number — this is why conjugates are useful when dividing.
conjugate of \(a + bi\)
\[\overline{a + bi} = a - bi\]
product (real!)
\[(a + bi)(a - bi) = a^2 + b^2\]
Multiply \(4 - 3i\) by its conjugate
\[(4 - 3i)(4 + 3i) = 16 + 12i - 12i - 9i^2\]
Cancel the middle and replace \(i^2\)
\[= 16 - 9(-1) = 16 + 9\]
Result — purely real
\[= 25\]
Example 6
\(\dfrac{2 + 3i}{4 - 2i}\)
Write the quotient in standard form. Standard form forbids \(i\) in the denominator — use the conjugate of the denominator to clear it.
Step 1 — Multiply by the conjugate over itself
\[\dfrac{2 + 3i}{4 - 2i} \cdot \dfrac{4 + 2i}{4 + 2i}\]
Step 2 — Expand the numerator
\[(2 + 3i)(4 + 2i) = 8 + 4i + 12i + 6i^2 = 8 + 16i - 6 = 2 + 16i\]
Step 3 — Expand the denominator
\[(4 - 2i)(4 + 2i) = 16 + 4 = 20\]
Step 4 — Split into real and imaginary parts
\[\dfrac{2 + 16i}{20} = \dfrac{2}{20} + \dfrac{16}{20}\,i\]
Standard form
\[\dfrac{1}{10} + \dfrac{4}{5}\,i\]
Example 7
Solve \(2x^2 + 50 = 0\)
Solve the quadratic equation. The answer will be a pair of pure imaginary numbers.
Step 1 — Isolate \(x^2\)
\[2x^2 = -50 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; x^2 = -25\]
Step 2 — Square-root both sides
\[x = \pm\sqrt{-25}\]
Step 3 — Pull out \(i\)
\[x = \pm\sqrt{25}\cdot\sqrt{-1} = \pm 5i\]
Solutions
\[x = 5i \quad\text{or}\quad x = -5i\]
Example 8
Solve \(5x^2 + 2x + 1 = 0\)
Use the Quadratic Formula. A negative discriminant signals complex solutions.
Step 1 — Apply the Quadratic Formula
\[x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} = \dfrac{-2 \pm \sqrt{4 - 20}}{10}\]
Step 2 — Simplify the discriminant
\[= \dfrac{-2 \pm \sqrt{-16}}{10}\]
Step 3 — Pull out \(i\)
\[= \dfrac{-2 \pm 4i}{10}\]
Step 4 — Split into real and imaginary parts
\[= -\dfrac{2}{10} \pm \dfrac{4}{10}\,i = -\dfrac{1}{5} \pm \dfrac{2}{5}\,i\]
Solutions (complex conjugate pair)
\[x = -\dfrac{1}{5} + \dfrac{2}{5}i \quad\text{or}\quad x = -\dfrac{1}{5} - \dfrac{2}{5}i\]
Example 9
Build a Quadratic with Zeros \(3 \pm 2i\)
Write a quadratic equation whose zeros are \(3 + 2i\) and \(3 - 2i\).
Step 1 — Convert zeros to factors
\[\bigl(x - (3 + 2i)\bigr)\bigl(x - (3 - 2i)\bigr)\]
Step 2 — Group \((x - 3)\) together
\[= \bigl((x - 3) - 2i\bigr)\bigl((x - 3) + 2i\bigr)\]
Step 3 — Apply \((A - B)(A + B) = A^2 - B^2\)
\[= (x - 3)^2 - (2i)^2\]
Step 4 — Expand and replace \(i^2 = -1\)
\[= x^2 - 6x + 9 - (-4) = x^2 - 6x + 13\]
Quadratic Equation
\[x^2 - 6x + 13 = 0\]
Review
Can You Now...?
Use this checklist to confirm you can work fluently with complex numbers.
Place the Number
Identify whether a given number is natural, whole, integer, rational, irrational, real, or complex — and know that all of them are complex.
Write Standard Form
Any complex number can be written as \(a + bi\). Always end your answer with the real part first.
Add & Subtract
Combine real parts with real parts and imaginary parts with imaginary parts. Treat \(i\) like a variable.
Multiply
Distribute (or FOIL), then replace every \(i^2\) with \(-1\). For powers of \(i\), use the period-4 pattern.
Divide via Conjugates
Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator. The new denominator is purely real.
Solve Quadratics
A negative discriminant produces a conjugate pair of complex zeros. Build a polynomial from complex zeros by multiplying the conjugate pair first.
Complex zeros of polynomials with real coefficients always come in conjugate pairs — if \(a + bi\) is a zero, so is \(a - bi\). This makes them easy to handle in factored form.
