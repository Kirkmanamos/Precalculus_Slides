3.1 Exponential Functions & Their Graphs
Objective: Recognize exponential growth vs. decay, graph parent and transformed exponential functions, work with the natural base \(e\), and apply compound interest.
growth vs. decay parent functions transformations natural base \(e\) compound interest
Big Idea
Exponential Growth vs. Exponential Decay
A quantity changes exponentially when its rate of change is proportional to how much is already there — bigger amounts grow (or shrink) faster.
Exponential Growth
A quantity grows exponentially when its rate of growth is proportional to what is already there.
all of the form \(y = b^x\) with \(b > 1\)
Exponential Decay
A quantity decays exponentially when its rate of decay is proportional to what is already there.
all of the form \(y = b^x\) with \(0 < b < 1\)

Transformations of Exponential Functions
\[f(x) = a\,(b)^{x - h} + k\]
\(h\) — Horizontal Translation
Shifts the graph right by \(h\) (or left if \(h\) is negative). The horizontal asymptote is unchanged.
\(k\) — Vertical Translation
Shifts the graph up by \(k\) (down if negative). The new horizontal asymptote is \(y = k\).
\(a < 0\)
Reflection across the \(x\)-axis (curve flips below the asymptote).
\(|a| > 1\)
Vertical stretch by factor \(|a|\).
\(0 < |a| < 1\)
Vertical compression by factor \(|a|\).

Example 3
Find \(f(x) = ab^x\) given \(f(1) = -6\) and \(f(2) = -18\)
Find the values of \(a\) and \(b\).
Set up two equations
\[ab^{1} = -6 \qquad \text{and} \qquad ab^{2} = -18\]
Divide to eliminate \(a\)
\[\frac{ab^{2}}{ab^{1}} = \frac{-18}{-6} \ \Longrightarrow\ b = 3\]
Back-substitute for \(a\)
\[a(3) = -6 \ \Longrightarrow\ a = -2\]
Answer
\[f(x) = -2 \cdot 3^{x}\]
Example 4
Find \(f(x) = ab^{-x} + c\) with HA \(y = 32\) through \((0, 122)\) and \((2, 72)\)
Find \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\).
Use the asymptote to find \(c\)
As \(x \to \infty\), \(b^{-x} \to 0\), so the HA is \(y = c\). Therefore \(c = 32\).
Use \((0, 122)\) to find \(a\)
\[a \cdot b^{0} + 32 = 122 \ \Longrightarrow\ a + 32 = 122 \ \Longrightarrow\ a = 90\]
Use \((2, 72)\) to find \(b\)
\[90\,b^{-2} + 32 = 72 \ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{90}{b^{2}} = 40 \ \Longrightarrow\ b^{2} = \tfrac{9}{4} \ \Longrightarrow\ b = \tfrac{3}{2}\]
Answer
\[f(x) = 90 \left(\tfrac{3}{2}\right)^{-x} + 32 \ =\ 90 \left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^{x} + 32\]
Definition
The Natural Base \(e\)
Natural Base
\(e = 2.7182818\ldots\)
An irrational, transcendental constant — the most "natural" base for exponential growth. It appears whenever a quantity grows continuously at a rate proportional to itself.
Natural Exponential Function
\[f(x) = e^{x}\]Same shape as any growth exponential, but its slope at every point equals its height.
Key Points on \(y = e^x\)
\[\left(-1,\ \tfrac{1}{e}\right),\quad (0,\,1),\quad (1,\,e)\]
Example 5
Let \(f(x) = e^{3x} - 2\) and \(g(x) = 2x^{3} + 3\). Find \((g \circ f)(0)\)
Evaluate the composition \(g(f(0))\).
Inner first — evaluate \(f(0)\)
\[f(0) = e^{3(0)} - 2 = e^{0} - 2 = 1 - 2 = -1\]
Now apply \(g\) to that output
\[g(-1) = 2(-1)^{3} + 3 = -2 + 3\]
Answer
\[(g \circ f)(0) = 1\]
Reference
Compound Interest
\(n\) Compoundings per Year
\[A = P\!\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}\]
Continuous Compounding
\[A = P\,e^{\,rt}\]
Variables
\(A\) = balance after \(t\) years  ·  \(P\) = principal  ·  \(r\) = annual rate (as a decimal)  ·  \(n\) = compoundings per year  ·  \(t\) = years
Connection
As \(n \to \infty\), the discrete formula \(\left(1 + \tfrac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}\) approaches the continuous one \(e^{rt}\). Continuous compounding is the "limit" of compounding more and more often.
Example 6
U.S. Population in 2040
The U.S. had a population of \(331.5\) million in 2020 and grows continuously at \(0.79\%\) per year, so \(P(t) = 331.5\,e^{0.0079t}\). Estimate the population in 2040.
Identify \(t\)
\(t\) is years since 2020, so \(2040 \Rightarrow t = 20\).
Substitute and compute
\[P(20) = 331.5\,e^{0.0079(20)} = 331.5\,e^{0.158}\]
Answer
\[P(20) \approx 331.5(1.1712) \approx 388.2 \text{ million}\]
Example 7
$12,000 invested at 3% for 4 years
Find the balance after \(4\) years if the interest is compounded (a) quarterly and (b) continuously.
Known values
\[P = 12000,\quad r = 0.03,\quad t = 4\]
(a) Quarterly — substitute \(n = 4\)
\[A = 12000\!\left(1 + \frac{0.03}{4}\right)^{4(4)} = 12000(1.0075)^{16}\]
(a) Quarterly answer
\[A \approx 12000(1.12700) \approx \$13{,}523.91\]
(b) Continuous — use \(A = P e^{rt}\)
\[A = 12000\,e^{(0.03)(4)} = 12000\,e^{0.12}\]
(b) Continuous answer
\[A \approx 12000(1.12750) \approx \$13{,}529.96\]
